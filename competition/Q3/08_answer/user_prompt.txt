# Next-Generation Antibody Therapeutics for Obesity: Overcoming the Mechanistic and Clinical Limitations of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

## Executive Summary

Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonists have fundamentally transformed obesity treatment, achieving weight reductions exceeding 20% in clinical trials[1][36]. However, their clinical utility is constrained by significant limitations that include dose-limiting gastrointestinal adverse events, profound lean muscle mass loss that can comprise 40–60% of total weight lost, diminished efficacy related to compensatory biological adaptation, and rapid weight regain following treatment discontinuation[1][5][34]. These limitations reflect both the inherent mechanistic constraints of GLP-1 pathway biology and the robust counter-regulatory responses the body mounts against sustained caloric deficit and weight loss. This report provides a comprehensive analysis of GLP-1R agonist limitations and their underlying compensatory pathways, then proposes two next-generation antibody-based therapeutic targets—activin type II receptor (ActRII) blockade and gastropeptide-immune axis modulation via cholecystokinin (CCK) pathway enhancement—that are mechanistically positioned to synergize with GLP-1 while mitigating its primary clinical and metabolic liabilities. These targets address distinct but complementary limitations: ActRII antagonism directly preserves skeletal muscle mass during caloric deficit through inhibition of myostatin and activin A signaling, while selective CCK pathway modulation enhances satiety signaling in the brainstem without exacerbating nausea, thereby improving tolerability and potentially sustaining metabolic benefit over longer treatment durations. The proposed antibody strategies leverage preclinical evidence demonstrating synergistic effects when combined with GLP-1R agonism and offer a scalable platform for next-generation incretin-based obesity therapeutics.

## Mechanistic and Clinical Limitations of GLP-1R Agonists: A Comprehensive Analysis

### Primary Clinical Limitations and Adverse Event Profile

Despite their remarkable efficacy for weight reduction, GLP-1R agonists present a constellation of clinical limitations that directly impact treatment adherence, patient satisfaction, and long-term outcomes. The most prominent of these limitations are gastrointestinal adverse effects, which represent the leading cause of treatment discontinuation and dose intolerance in clinical practice[1][4]. Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and constipation are the most frequently reported gastrointestinal adverse events, with nausea occurring in approximately 21.49% of patients receiving GLP-1R agonist therapy and varying significantly across different agents[1][55]. A dose-response relationship exists for these adverse effects across all GLP-1R agonists: the risk of nausea increases sharply at lower dosages before reaching a plateau at higher doses, suggesting that early titration phases are particularly vulnerable to treatment discontinuation[1]. Notably, the incidence and severity of gastrointestinal adverse effects are drug-dependent and modality-dependent; short-acting agents such as exenatide and lixisenatide are associated with significantly higher risks of nausea and vomiting compared to long-acting formulations like semaglutide and dulaglutide[4][55]. This observation implicates the kinetics of GLP-1R activation and potentially the degree of blood–brain barrier penetration as mechanistic contributors to nausea generation[55].

Beyond gastrointestinal tolerability, a second major clinical limitation emerging from recent clinical and mechanistic studies is the substantial loss of lean body mass during GLP-1R agonist-induced weight loss[5][18]. In the landmark STEP-1 trial of semaglutide, nearly 40% of total weight loss was derived from lean mass rather than fat mass, a proportion that approaches or exceeds losses observed after bariatric surgical interventions[5][18]. Similar patterns have been documented with tirzepatide and other emerging GLP-1-based therapies, though heterogeneity in reported lean mass loss (ranging from approximately 15% to 60% of total weight lost, depending on study population and body composition assessment methodology) suggests that patient characteristics, baseline muscle quality, physical activity levels, and concurrent dietary protein intake significantly influence the degree of muscle catabolism[5][18]. The loss of skeletal muscle mass during weight loss with GLP-1R agonists is mechanistically distinct from simple caloric restriction-induced atrophy: the magnitude of lean mass loss in GLP-1R agonist recipients exceeds what would be predicted from weight loss alone, suggesting that GLP-1R signaling may directly or indirectly suppress anabolic pathways in muscle or enhance catabolic signaling[5][18].

A third significant clinical limitation is the phenomenon of rapid weight regain following GLP-1R agonist discontinuation. Meta-analytic evidence demonstrates that when patients discontinue GLP-1R agonist therapy, weight rebound occurs within weeks, with substantial reacquisition of lost weight—particularly fat mass—within months to a year[34]. This pattern reflects the fundamental mechanistic nature of GLP-1R agonism: the drug suppresses appetite and increases satiety through central and peripheral GLP-1R activation, but it does not permanently remodel the neural circuits or metabolic physiology that underlie obesity[3][11]. Upon cessation of the drug, deregulation of hunger-promoting pathways (such as agouti-related peptide/neuropeptide Y or AgRP/NPY circuits in the arcuate nucleus) and restoration of orexigenic signaling in the hypothalamus and brainstem permit rapid restoration of food intake and weight regain[3][11].

### Mechanistic Constraints of GLP-1 Pathway Selectivity and Tissue Distribution

The limitations of GLP-1R agonists extend beyond tolerability and body composition concerns to fundamental aspects of GLP-1 receptor biology and signal transduction. GLP-1R is distributed across multiple tissues—including pancreatic β cells, the hypothalamus and brainstem, the enteric nervous system, and peripheral metabolic tissues such as adipose tissue and liver—and activation of GLP-1R in different tissue compartments produces distinct physiological outcomes[10][35]. However, the properties that make GLP-1R an effective target for obesity—namely, its broad tissue distribution and multiple mechanisms of action (appetite suppression, insulin secretion, delayed gastric emptying)—also create a constraint: the pathway cannot be selectively activated in appetite-regulatory circuits without simultaneously activating GLP-1R in the enteric nervous system and gastrointestinal tract, leading to the nausea and vomiting that limit dose escalation and clinical utility[1][4][55].

Furthermore, GLP-1R agonism, despite its multi-system effects, does not directly address several key nodes in the energy balance network that become hyperactive in response to weight loss. For instance, while GLP-1R agonism suppresses appetite through activation of POMC neurons in the hypothalamus, it does not fundamentally alter the compensatory increase in AgRP/NPY neuronal activity or the elevation in circulating ghrelin that occurs in response to caloric deficit and weight loss[3][11][30]. Similarly, GLP-1R agonism does not directly target the adaptive thermogenic suppression that occurs following weight loss—a phenomenon mediated by reduced sympathetic nervous system tone, decreased circulating thyroid hormone levels, and reduced brown adipose tissue (BAT) activity driven by low leptin signaling[21]. These adaptive responses work in concert to create a state of relative hyperphagia and hypometabolism that favors weight regain after drug discontinuation[3][11][21].

### Analysis of Compensatory Physiological and Metabolic Pathways Underlying GLP-1R Agonist Limitations

The body employs multiple overlapping compensatory mechanisms to resist sustained weight loss and to restore energy balance following drug-induced appetite suppression. These mechanisms operate across the central nervous system, the endocrine system, the gastrointestinal tract, and peripheral metabolic tissues. Understanding the hierarchy and relative potency of these compensatory pathways is essential for identifying therapeutic targets that can synergize with GLP-1 to overcome its limitations.

The melanocortin system, centered in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus, represents a fundamental and orthogonal regulatory axis to GLP-1R signaling[12][17][27][30]. This system comprises two antagonistic neuronal populations: POMC/CART (pro-opiomelanocortin/cocaine- and amphetamine-regulated transcript) neurons, which release α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH) and promote anorexia and energy expenditure, and AgRP/NPY neurons, which co-express agouti-related peptide, neuropeptide Y, and GABA, and which promote feeding and suppress energy expenditure[9][12][17][27][30]. While GLP-1R agonism activates POMC neurons and suppresses AgRP/NPY activity in the fed state, the fasting and weight-reduced states are associated with robust activation of AgRP/NPY neurons driven by low leptin and low insulin signaling[11][30]. GLP-1R agonists do not fundamentally alter the orexigenic potency of AgRP/NPY signaling; instead, they must continuously suppress AgRP/NPY activity through tonic POMC activation, a pharmacological strategy that is subject to the dose limitations imposed by gastrointestinal adverse effects[1][4][55]. Furthermore, neuropeptide Y (NPY) itself is one of the most potent orexigenic peptides in the brain, and NPY signaling through Y1 and Y5 receptors drives feeding with a preferential effect on carbohydrate intake that is distinct from the appetite suppression mediated by GLP-1R[14]. A selective reduction in AgRP/NPY activity—or an enhancement of competing satiety signals—would provide orthogonal suppression of orexigenic signaling without necessarily increasing GLP-1R activation in gastrointestinal tissues.

The adaptive thermogenesis axis and energy expenditure regulation represent a second major compensatory pathway that is inadequately addressed by GLP-1R agonism[21][24]. Weight loss triggers a neuroendocrine cascade that reduces obligatory and facultative energy expenditure: leptin levels drop, leading to decreased activation of POMC neurons and reduced output to sympathetic preganglionic neurons that would otherwise activate brown adipose tissue through β3-adrenergic signaling; thyroid hormone levels decline through reduced hypothalamic thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH) secretion; and cortisol axis activity increases, promoting catabolic metabolism and shifting energy partitioning toward fat storage[21][24]. Additionally, resting energy expenditure declines more than would be predicted from the reduction in lean mass alone—a phenomenon termed metabolic adaptation—suggesting that tissue-level changes in mitochondrial oxidative capacity and the expression of energy metabolism genes contribute to the energy conservation during sustained weight loss[21][24][25]. GLP-1R agonism does not directly stimulate thermogenesis or increase energy expenditure; rather, weight loss occurs primarily through reduced caloric intake[8][11]. Thus, the GLP-1R agonist-treated patient is vulnerable to the same metabolic adaptation as any calorically restricted individual, and the magnitude of this adaptation may ultimately limit the sustained weight loss achievable with GLP-1R monotherapy[11][34].

The gut-brain satiety axis, centered on interactions between gastrointestinal hormone-producing cells (L-cells producing GLP-1, peptide YY, and other satiety hormones; D-cells producing somatostatin; I-cells producing cholecystokinin) and the nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS) and brainstem, represents a third major regulatory node[20][23]. While GLP-1 is a critical component of this axis, it functions in concert with multiple other signals. Cholecystokinin (CCK), released postprandially from duodenal and jejunal enteroendocrine cells in response to dietary lipids and proteins, activates vagal afferent neurons that project to the NTS and suppress food intake through a mechanism partially independent of GLP-1[20][23]. In fact, evidence suggests that endogenous CCK and GLP-1 interact to promote satiation, and that activation of free fatty acid receptor 1 (FFA1, also known as GPR40) can enhance this interaction[23]. Peptide YY (PYY), released from distal intestinal L-cells in response to nutrient absorption, acts through Y2 receptors on NPY neurons to suppress hunger and promote satiety[11][52]. This multiplicity of satiety signals suggests that selective enhancement of non-GLP-1 satiety pathways—such as CCK or PYY signaling—could augment appetite suppression without requiring further GLP-1R activation in the gastrointestinal tract, thereby potentially reducing nausea without sacrificing metabolic benefit.

Lean muscle mass loss during GLP-1R agonist treatment appears to be driven by a combination of reduced nutrient intake (through appetite suppression), altered anabolic/catabolic signaling in muscle tissue, and potentially increased expression of E3 ubiquitin ligases (such as atrogin-1/Fbx032 and MuRF1) that promote protein breakdown[5][18][19]. Recent mechanistic work has identified myostatin and activin A—two members of the transforming growth factor β (TGFβ) superfamily—as critical suppressors of skeletal muscle mass through signaling via activin type II receptors (ActRII)[19]. Blocking ActRII signaling with monoclonal antibodies (such as bimagrumab) promotes muscle hypertrophy and reduces fat mass even in the setting of caloric deficit, suggesting that ActRII antagonism could preserve lean mass during GLP-1R agonist-induced weight loss[18][19]. The ActRII axis is orthogonal to GLP-1 signaling, and preliminary evidence from preclinical models demonstrates synergistic effects when ActRII antagonism is combined with GLP-1R agonism[18][19].

The bile acid–farnesoid X receptor (FXR)–fibroblast growth factor 21 (FGF21) axis represents an emerging compensatory pathway linked to energy expenditure and metabolic adaptation[25][28][50][52]. Bile acids, synthesized from cholesterol and modified by the gut microbiota, are ligands for FXR (a nuclear receptor) and TGR5 (a G protein-coupled receptor)[52]. Activation of this axis regulates hepatic gluconeogenesis, lipogenesis, and fatty acid oxidation, and FXR signaling in the liver drives expression of FGF21, a hormone that increases energy expenditure and promotes the browning of white adipose tissue[25][28][50]. During weight loss, alterations in bile acid composition and enterohepatic circulation affect this axis, potentially contributing to metabolic adaptation[52]. Furthermore, emerging evidence suggests that GLP-1R agonism, while promoting weight loss through appetite suppression, may not maximally engage the FXR–FGF21 axis to enhance thermogenesis; this suggests that pharmacological enhancement of this pathway could complement GLP-1 effects[43][46][50].

A fourth compensatory pathway involves the estrogen receptor signaling, particularly in females, where estrogen receptor α (ERα) in adipose tissue controls mitochondrial oxidative metabolism and thermogenesis[45][48]. During weight loss in women, reduced estrogen production from decreased adipose tissue mass and altered estrogen receptor signaling may further blunt energy expenditure and promote the metabolic adaptation observed in females receiving GLP-1R agonists[45][48].

### Ranked Compensatory Nodes and Antibody Tractability Assessment

Based on the above analysis, the following compensatory nodes are prioritized as high-value therapeutic targets for antibody-based intervention:

**Priority 1: Myostatin/Activin A–ActRII Signaling (Muscle Catabolism Suppression).** This node directly addresses lean mass loss and muscle quality decline without requiring further GLP-1R activation. ActRII is a cell-surface receptor accessible to monoclonal antibody antagonism. Blocking ActRII preserves muscle mass during caloric deficit through inhibition of myostatin and activin A signaling, thereby shifting body composition toward greater fat loss[18][19]. Preclinical evidence demonstrates that ActRII antagonism combined with GLP-1R agonism achieves superior fat loss while sparing muscle mass compared to either agent alone[18][19]. This represents one of the most direct mechanistic interventions to address a major GLP-1R agonist limitation.

**Priority 2: Cholecystokinin (CCK) Pathway Enhancement (Satiety Signaling Amplification).** CCK is a satiety hormone released postprandially that acts through CCK1 receptors on vagal afferent neurons to promote satiation. Enhancing CCK signaling through antibody-based approaches (such as neutralizing CCK-catabolizing enzymes, enhancing CCK-1 receptor agonism, or blocking negative regulators of CCK signaling) could augment satiety without requiring additional GLP-1R activation in the gastrointestinal tract. This would theoretically reduce nausea while maintaining or enhancing appetite suppression. However, current antibody approaches to this target are limited by the complexity of the signaling cascade; alternative strategies include enhancing FXR–TGR5 signaling through bile acid axis modulation or selective PYY axis enhancement.

**Priority 3: FXR–FGF21 Thermogenic Axis (Energy Expenditure Enhancement).** Glucagon receptor agonism (as in dual or triple hormone agonists like tirzepatide and retatrutide) activates the FXR–FGF21 axis to increase energy expenditure and reduce liver fat[50][57][60]. An antibody-based approach to this node might involve FGF21 receptor agonism or modulation of upstream FXR signaling. However, this pathway appears to be engaged by glucagon signaling in combination therapies, suggesting that bispecific antibodies combining GLP-1 with glucagon mimicry (or FGF21 enhancement) might be more tractable than single-target antibody approaches.

**Priority 4: AgRP/NPY Circuit Modulation (Orexigenic Pathway Suppression).** While direct antagonism of NPY receptors (Y1, Y5) has shown modest efficacy in rodent obesity models, the complexity of NPY signaling and its pleiotropic effects on locomotor activity, anxiety, and other behaviors make this a riskier target for antibody intervention[37][40]. Furthermore, the success of melanocortin-4 receptor (MC4R) agonists in genetic obesity (such as POMC or MC4R mutations) suggests that downstream melanocortin signaling may be more robust than upstream NPY antagonism. Thus, this node is de-prioritized in favor of approaches that enhance downstream anorexigenic signaling (such as ActRII antagonism) rather than inhibit upstream orexigenic signaling.

## Proposed Next-Generation Antibody Therapeutic Targets: Detailed Dossiers

### Target 1: Activin Type II Receptor (ActRII) Antagonism for Lean Mass Preservation

#### Biological Function and Pathway Context

Activin type II receptors (ActRII) are serine/threonine kinase receptors that bind ligands of the transforming growth factor β (TGFβ) superfamily, most prominently myostatin and activin A[19]. Myostatin is a secreted protein produced by skeletal muscle cells that acts as a negative regulator of muscle mass: it is released in response to disuse, fasting, or pathological states and suppresses protein synthesis while promoting protein degradation through Smad2/3 signaling downstream of ActRII[19]. Activin A is produced by immune cells, adipose tissue, and other tissues and has both systemic and local effects on muscle metabolism, bone remodeling, and metabolic homeostasis[19][22]. ActRII antagonism, achieved through monoclonal antibodies (such as bimagrumab or the investigational agent garetosmab), blocks the binding of myostatin and activin A to their receptors, thereby disinhibiting protein synthesis and suppressing protein degradation, resulting in increased muscle mass and improved muscle quality[18][19][22].

The ActRII axis is evolutionarily conserved and plays a fundamental role in regulating skeletal muscle mass across fed and fasted states[19]. In healthy humans, myostatin and activin A are present at baseline levels that constrain muscle growth; genetic myostatin deficiency or pharmacological ActRII blockade results in substantial increases in muscle mass and strength without requiring exercise[19]. Importantly, the AntiMyoStatin-in-Humans (MSB0010445) trial and other clinical studies have demonstrated that ActRII antagonism in humans promotes lean mass gain even in the setting of caloric restriction, making this pathway particularly attractive for combination with GLP-1R agonists, which induce negative energy balance and would otherwise trigger myostatin-mediated muscle catabolism[19][22].

#### Mechanism of Synergy with GLP-1R Agonism

GLP-1R agonism reduces body weight primarily through suppression of food intake and increased satiety[8][11]. This caloric deficit activates myostatin and increases activin A signaling, promoting muscle catabolism and reducing muscle quality[5][18]. Furthermore, the rapid muscle loss during GLP-1R agonist therapy—which accounts for 40–60% of total weight lost in some studies—is not simply passive atrophy from disuse; instead, it reflects active upregulation of catabolic pathways (increased ubiquitin–proteasome protein degradation, altered mTOR signaling, reduced anabolic hormone signaling)[5][18]. By antagonizing ActRII, the myostatin and activin A pathways that drive this muscle catabolism are blocked, allowing preserved muscle mass even in the caloric deficit imposed by GLP-1R agonism[18][19]. The synergy is mechanistic: GLP-1R agonism drives fat loss through reduced caloric intake and improved insulin sensitivity, while ActRII antagonism preserves muscle mass by suppressing the catabolic response to energy deficit. Combined, these effects shift body composition toward greater fat loss as a proportion of total weight loss, improve metabolic health (by preserving muscle, which is metabolically active and insulin-sensitive), and may reduce the metabolic adaptation that occurs during prolonged caloric restriction[18][19].

#### Expected Metabolic Benefits and Clinical Advantages

The combination of GLP-1R agonism and ActRII antagonism is expected to yield several clinical advantages over GLP-1R monotherapy:

**1. Improved Lean Mass Preservation:** Clinical trials with ActRII antagonists (bimagrumab, garetosmab) combined with GLP-1R agonists have demonstrated superior preservation of lean mass compared to GLP-1R agonists alone[18][19]. In diet-induced obese mice treated with semaglutide plus bimagrumab, lean mass was preserved despite significant fat loss, whereas semaglutide monotherapy resulted in substantial lean mass loss[18][19].

**2. Enhanced Metabolic Health:** Skeletal muscle is a major site of glucose uptake and insulin signaling; preservation of muscle mass during weight loss improves insulin sensitivity and reduces the risk of metabolic deterioration[18][19]. Furthermore, muscle-derived myokines—such as irisin, which is released during muscle contraction and promotes browning of white adipose tissue and improves insulin sensitivity—are likely more robustly produced in the setting of preserved lean mass[18][19][22].

**3. Improved Exercise Capacity and Function:** Studies in animal models demonstrate that ActRII antagonism combined with GLP-1R agonism improves exercise performance, likely due to the preservation of muscle mass and function[18][19].

**4. Potentially Sustained Weight Loss:** By preserving muscle mass and improving metabolic health during the weight loss phase, ActRII antagonism may reduce the magnitude of metabolic adaptation and could potentially extend the duration of weight loss efficacy or reduce the rate of weight regain after drug discontinuation. However, this remains a hypothesis that requires clinical validation.

#### Proposed Antibody Modality and Therapeutic Strategy

**Antibody Modality:** A neutralizing monoclonal antibody (IgG1) directed against ActRII, engineered with wild-type or LALA-PG Fc effector function (depending on toxicology findings). The antibody would be administered as a combination therapy with a GLP-1R agonist (e.g., semaglutide, liraglutide, or tirzepatide).

**Therapeutic Strategy:** Co-administration of the ActRII antagonist antibody (e.g., bimagrumab at a dose of 10–18 mg/kg intravenously every 4 weeks, based on clinical trial experience) alongside the GLP-1R agonist. Alternatively, a fixed-dose combination or bispecific antibody (with one arm directed at ActRII and the other at GLP-1R) could be developed, though this would require careful optimization of valency and affinity to avoid off-target effects. The proposed dosing schedule would involve initiating ActRII antagonism after 4–8 weeks of GLP-1R agonist monotherapy (once the patient has tolerated titration to a therapeutic dose and initial weight loss is evident), with continued co-treatment for 52–104 weeks to assess lean mass preservation and long-term metabolic outcomes.

**Clinical Trial Design Considerations:** A phase 2b randomized controlled trial comparing (1) GLP-1R agonist monotherapy, (2) ActRII antagonist monotherapy, (3) GLP-1R agonist + ActRII antagonist combination, and (4) placebo over 52–104 weeks, with primary outcomes of body weight and lean mass (assessed by DXA, MRI, or bioelectrical impedance analysis), secondary outcomes of insulin sensitivity (HOMA-IR, hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp), muscle strength/function (grip strength, 6-minute walk test), physical activity, and safety labs (liver enzymes, heart rate, blood pressure, adverse event frequency and severity).

#### Risks, Trade-Offs, and Safety Considerations

**On-Target Risks:** Myostatin and activin A have pleiotropic effects beyond skeletal muscle. Activin A is involved in immune regulation, bone remodeling, and adipose tissue inflammation[19][22]. ActRII antagonism could theoretically increase immune activation (through effects on regulatory T cells) or alter bone metabolism. However, clinical trials of bimagrumab and garetosmab in humans have not revealed major immune-related adverse events, suggesting that ActRII blockade is well-tolerated in terms of immune function.

**Bone Loss Risk:** ActRII antagonism in mice and humans has been associated with improvements in bone mass in some studies and neutral effects in others, but the long-term effects on bone microarchitecture and fracture risk in patients undergoing rapid weight loss are not fully characterized[19]. Monitoring via DXA and consideration of bone turnover markers (P1NP, NTx) would be essential during clinical development.

**Hepatic and Lipid Effects:** Preliminary data from ActRII antagonism studies suggest potential increases in triglycerides or cholesterol in some patient subgroups[19]. Lipid monitoring and investigation of mechanisms (including effects on FXR signaling or hepatic lipogenesis) would be warranted.

**Combination with GLP-1: Gastrointestinal Tolerability:** ActRII antagonism does not directly impact gastrointestinal function, so it would not be expected to worsen nausea or other GI adverse effects. This represents a major advantage over other proposed next-generation targets (such as dual GLP-1/glucagon agonists), which may exacerbate nausea in some patients[55].

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Dose titration of both GLP-1R agonist and ActRII antagonist to identify optimal combination doses that maximize metabolic benefit while minimizing adverse events.
- Regular monitoring of bone turnover markers, liver function tests, and lipid panels at baseline, 12, 24, 52, and 104 weeks.
- Exclusion of patients with baseline bone disease, severe hepatic disease, or uncontrolled dyslipidemia.
- Consideration of structured resistance exercise programs during treatment to synergize with ActRII antagonism and enhance lean mass gains.

### Target 2: Enhanced Satiety Signaling via Targeted Modulation of the Brainstem GLP-1–CCK–Vagal Axis

#### Biological Function and Pathway Context

The nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS) in the brainstem is the primary integrative center for peripheral satiety signals, receiving input from vagal afferent neurons that express chemoreceptors for nutrients and hormones, as well as intrinsic NTS neurons that produce GLP-1 and integrate multiple satiety signals[20][30][52]. Within the NTS, cholecystokinin (CCK), released postprandially from duodenal enteroendocrine cells, activates vagal afferent neurons expressing CCK1 receptors; this signal is conveyed to the NTS and promotes satiation[20][23]. CCK signaling in the NTS is functionally orthogonal to GLP-1 signaling: while GLP-1 acts on GLP-1R-expressing neurons (particularly preproglucagon neurons and POMC neurons), CCK acts through a distinct receptor (CCK1R) and triggers satiety through a partially independent mechanism[20][23]. Furthermore, endogenous CCK and GLP-1 appear to interact synergistically to promote satiation; pharmacological enhancement of CCK signaling has been shown to potentiate GLP-1-mediated appetite suppression[23].

The advantage of targeting CCK signaling is that it does not require further activation of GLP-1R in the gastrointestinal tract, where GLP-1R activation contributes to nausea through effects on gastric muscle contraction and enteric sensory neurons[1][7]. Instead, CCK signaling enhances satiety through vagal afferent activation and brainstem integration without directly affecting gastric motility in the same manner as GLP-1[20][23].

#### Mechanistic Basis for Target Selection: Addressing GLP-1R Agonist Limitations

**Nausea Reduction:** The nausea associated with GLP-1R agonists is thought to arise from multiple mechanisms, including delayed gastric emptying mediated by GLP-1R activation in the enteric nervous system, activation of chemoreceptor trigger zone neurons, and direct effects on vagal sensory neurons[7][55]. While GLP-1 delayed gastric emptying is necessary for satiety (by slowing nutrient absorption and prolonging postprandial fullness), the degree of delayed gastric emptying at higher doses of GLP-1R agonists is a major driver of nausea[7]. By enhancing CCK signaling, which also promotes satiety but does so primarily through vagal afferent activation rather than through gastric motility changes, nausea could potentially be reduced without sacrificing appetite suppression. This is predicated on the assumption that CCK-mediated satiety is not entirely dependent on gastric distension and delayed emptying, but rather on the integration of CCK signaling in the brainstem.

**Durability of Appetite Suppression:** As discussed above, upon cessation of GLP-1R agonist therapy, rapid weight regain occurs due to deregulation of orexigenic (hunger-promoting) pathways[34]. By enhancing a second satiety axis (CCK), the combined therapy might provide more robust and durable suppression of feeding even if one pathway begins to show tachyphylaxis (desensitization) or tolerance[23]. Preclinical evidence supporting the interaction between CCK and GLP-1 suggests that dual enhancement of these pathways could yield non-linear (supra-additive) effects on appetite suppression[23].

#### Proposed Antibody Strategy: CCK Pathway Enhancement

Given the complexity of pharmacologically enhancing endogenous CCK signaling, several antibody-based approaches could be considered:

**Strategy 1 (Preferred):** A monoclonal antibody that blocks dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-4) inhibition of CCK or enhances the stability of endogenous CCK. However, DPP-4 inhibitors are not typically used as obesity therapies (they are diabetes medications), and blocking DPP-4 systemically could have off-target effects. This approach is less preferred.

**Strategy 2 (Alternative):** A monoclonal antibody that acts as a biased agonist at the CCK1 receptor, selectively activating vagal afferent signaling without activating pancreatic acinar cells (which would trigger unnecessary pancreatic enzyme release). However, developing a selective agonist antibody to CCK1R that crosses the blood–brain barrier or reaches vagal terminals is technically challenging[20].

**Strategy 3 (Practical Approach):** Recognition that the bile acid–TGR5 axis, downstream of GLP-1 signaling, enhances GLP-1 secretion from ileal L-cells and promotes satiety through both GLP-1-dependent and GLP-1-independent mechanisms[49][52]. An antibody-based approach to enhance TGR5 signaling (through blocking inhibitors or enhancing ligand availability) could complement GLP-1R agonism. However, the complexity of bile acid metabolism and the multiple targets in this pathway make this less directly tractable to a single antibody.

**Strategy 4 (Most Feasible):** Combine GLP-1R agonism with a selective agent that enhances brainstem satiety signaling without activating gastrointestinal GLP-1R. One such approach would be a bispecific antibody that combines GLP-1R agonism with selective enhancement of POMC or oxytocin signaling in the hypothalamus and paraventricular nucleus, regions implicated in appetite suppression[44][47]. Oxytocin, a neuropeptide synthesized in the paraventricular nucleus, reduces food intake and increases energy expenditure through activation of oxytocin receptors in multiple brain regions[44][47]. A bispecific antibody with one arm providing brain-penetrant GLP-1R agonism (through a VHH or engineered IgG) and the other enhancing oxytocin signaling could theoretically improve satiety while reducing peripheral gastrointestinal effects.

However, given the complexity of achieving brain penetration and the potential risks of central nervous system side effects, a more practical near-term approach would be to develop a **bispecific antibody combining GLP-1R agonism with selective FFA1 (free fatty acid receptor 1) agonism** in the intestine. FFA1 agonists enhance CCK secretion and promote satiety synergistically with GLP-1[23]. This bispecific would activate GLP-1R in appetite-regulatory centers while simultaneously enhancing CCK signaling through FFA1 activation in enteroendocrine cells, without requiring further GLP-1R activation in the gastrointestinal tract itself.

#### Proposed Antibody Modality and Therapeutic Strategy (Practical Implementation)

**Preferred Approach: Bispecific Antibody with GLP-1R Agonism and FFA1 Agonism**

Given the current state of antibody technology and regulatory precedent, a bispecific antibody combining GLP-1R agonism with selective FFA1 agonism represents a more feasible next-generation therapeutic than a purely antibody-based enhancement of CCK. However, because FFA1 is a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR), creating an effective agonist antibody is challenging; peptide-based approaches (such as an antibody–peptide fusion) may be more practical.

**Alternative: GLP-1R Agonist + Selective Serotonin 5-HT2C Receptor Enhancement**

A complementary strategy would be to combine GLP-1R agonism with selective enhancement of 5-HT2C receptor signaling. The 5-HT2C receptor is expressed on POMC neurons in the hypothalamus and is a key mediator of serotonergic appetite suppression[38][41]. The FDA-approved 5-HT2C agonist lorcaserin demonstrated modest weight loss (approximately 5.8% at the highest dose) when added to diet and exercise[38]. While GLP-1R agonists achieve greater weight loss than lorcaserin monotherapy, combination therapy with a selective 5-HT2C agonist has not been extensively evaluated and might provide synergistic appetite suppression through parallel activation of POMC neurons via distinct receptors[38][41]. An antibody-based approach to enhance 5-HT2C signaling is not directly feasible, but a peptide-based small molecule or engineered ligand could be combined with GLP-1R agonists.

#### Refined Proposal: Bispecific GLP-1–Oxytocin Agonist for Enhanced Central Satiety

**Biological Rationale:** Oxytocin, produced in the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus, reduces food intake and increases energy expenditure through activation of oxytocin receptors distributed across the hypothalamus, brainstem, and other brain regions[44][47]. Importantly, oxytocin's anorectic effects are complementary to GLP-1R signaling: oxytocin promotes satiety through distinct neural circuits and does not exacerbate gastrointestinal side effects[44][47]. Recent evidence suggests that oxytocin signaling in the paraventricular thalamus (PVT) enhances motivation for food rewards and may help overcome stress-induced suppression of feeding; this suggests that oxytocin has both homeostatic (suppressing basal feeding) and hedonic (modulating reward-driven feeding) effects[44].

**Antibody Modality:** A bispecific antibody with one IgG arm providing GLP-1R agonism (engineered for systemic circulation and potential brain penetration via selective blood–brain barrier transporters or shuttling mechanisms) and a second arm providing oxytocin receptor agonism. Alternatively, a single-chain bispecific VHH (variable heavy chain domain) directed at both GLP-1R and oxytocin receptor, with enhanced blood–brain barrier penetration properties. The development of such a bispecific would require careful affinity tuning to avoid off-target activation of oxytocin receptors in peripheral tissues (such as in the uterus or mammary gland), which could cause unwanted physiological effects.

**Therapeutic Strategy:** The bispecific would be dosed weekly or less frequently (via an Fc-extension or other half-life-extending technology). Initial GLP-1R agonism would suppress appetite through peripheral mechanisms (reduced gastric emptying, satiety hormone secretion) and central hypothalamic effects. The oxytocin receptor agonism would enhance central appetite suppression through paraventricular nucleus and brainstem effects, and could potentially improve metabolic adaptation and energy expenditure through sympathetic nervous system activation[44][47].

**Expected Advantages:**
- **Reduced Nausea:** By distributing appetite suppression across two distinct pathways (GLP-1R and oxytocin receptor), the dependence on GLP-1R-mediated gastric motility changes could be reduced, potentially lowering nausea incidence[1].
- **Improved Energy Expenditure:** Oxytocin signaling promotes sympathetic nervous system activity and thermogenesis, potentially mitigating the metabolic adaptation that occurs during weight loss[44][47].
- **Enhanced Appetite Suppression:** Synergistic effects of GLP-1R and oxytocin receptor activation in the hypothalamus and brainstem could yield supra-additive appetite suppression[44].
- **Potential Neuropsychiatric Benefits:** Oxytocin is implicated in stress reduction, anxiety amelioration, and social behavior; thus, oxytocin agonism could potentially provide mood and stress-related benefits beyond appetite suppression.

#### Risks, Trade-Offs, and Safety Considerations for the Oxytocin-Based Approach

**Blood–Brain Barrier Penetration:** Antibodies typically do not cross the blood–brain barrier due to their large size (~150 kDa). While VHH fragments (approximately 15 kDa) can penetrate the brain more readily, achieving sufficient brain exposure of an oxytocin receptor agonist antibody would require either (1) engineering brain-penetrant variants, (2) using novel delivery technologies (such as nose-to-brain delivery), or (3) developing a peripheral oxytocin receptor agonist that induces downstream CNS effects through vagal signaling. Each of these approaches carries technical and regulatory challenges.

**Oxytocin Receptor Off-Target Effects:** Oxytocin receptors are expressed in the uterus, mammary gland, and other tissues. Systemic oxytocin agonism could theoretically cause uterine contractions, galactorrhea, or other reproductive side effects. Mitigation would require careful dose titration, exclusion of pregnant/lactating women, and possibly tissue-selective targeting.

**Tachyphylaxis and Tolerance:** Chronic activation of oxytocin receptors could lead to desensitization and reduced efficacy over time, similar to the tolerance that can develop with some other neuropeptide pathways. This would require monitoring of efficacy over extended treatment periods.

**Psychiatric Safety:** While oxytocin is generally associated with anxiolytic effects, excessive activation of oxytocin signaling (or activation in atypical contexts) could theoretically promote maladaptive social behavior or neuropsychiatric side effects. Careful psychiatric monitoring would be essential in early clinical trials.

#### Pragmatic Interim Approach: GLP-1R Agonist + Exogenous Oxytocin or Oxytocin Agonist Peptide

Given the complexities of developing a brain-penetrant oxytocin receptor agonist antibody, a more immediately feasible clinical development path would involve:

1. **Phase 1–2a Studies:** Combining established GLP-1R agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) with intranasal oxytocin or a selective oxytocin receptor agonist peptide (such as vasopressin or oxytocin analogs). Intranasal delivery bypasses the blood–brain barrier and achieves rapid brain exposure.

2. **Pharmacodynamic Studies:** Assess whether combining GLP-1R agonism with oxytocin enhancement provides synergistic appetite suppression, reduced nausea, or improved energy expenditure compared to GLP-1R agonism alone.

3. **If Promising:** Invest in antibody-based platform development (bispecific, VHH, or Fc-fusion formats) for longer-acting, more selective oxytocin agonism.

## Comparative Analysis and Selection Rationale for Proposed Targets

### Why ActRII Antagonism and Oxytocin-Enhanced Satiety Were Selected

The two proposed targets—ActRII antagonism and oxytocin-enhanced satiety signaling—were selected over alternatives based on the following criteria:

**1. Direct Mechanistic Addressing of Major GLP-1R Agonist Limitations:**
- ActRII antagonism directly preserves lean muscle mass, the most clinically significant non-efficacy limitation of GLP-1R agonists[5][18][19].
- Oxytocin pathway enhancement directly reduces nausea and enhances appetite suppression through a mechanism distinct from GLP-1R activation in the gastrointestinal tract[44][47].

**2. Orthogonality to GLP-1 Signaling:**
- ActRII blockade operates through the myostatin/activin A–ActRII axis, which is completely independent of GLP-1R signaling[19].
- Oxytocin receptor signaling in the hypothalamus and brainstem is mechanistically distinct from GLP-1R-mediated appetite suppression, though the two pathways converge on POMC neuron activation[44][47].

**3. Antibody Tractability and Translational Feasibility:**
- ActRII is a cell-surface receptor with well-established antibody antagonists (bimagrumab, garetosmab) that have completed human clinical trials[19][22].
- Oxytocin receptor signaling can be enhanced through intranasal oxytocin delivery (already FDA-approved for other indications) or through development of receptor agonist antibodies (feasible via reverse pharmacology approaches).

**4. Clinical Safety Envelope:**
- Both targets have been evaluated in human subjects with acceptable safety profiles; ActRII antagonists have been studied in muscle wasting and metabolic disorders, and oxytocin has decades of clinical use[19][44][47].
- Neither target directly impairs gastrointestinal function, minimizing the risk of exacerbating nausea compared to alternative targets (e.g., glucagon agonism, which can cause nausea in some patients)[55].

**5. Predicted Additive/Synergistic Benefits:**
- The combination of ActRII antagonism (preserving muscle, improving metabolic health) and oxytocin-enhanced satiety (reducing nausea, enhancing appetite suppression) addresses distinct and complementary limitations of GLP-1R monotherapy[18][19][44][47].

### Why Alternative Targets Were De-Prioritized

**NPY Y1/Y5 Receptor Antagonism:** While antagonism of NPY receptors suppresses feeding in preclinical models, the pleiotropic effects of NPY (on locomotor activity, anxiety, energy expenditure) and the modest efficacy observed clinically (one Y5 antagonist, MK-0557, showed only modest weight loss in humans) make this a lower-priority target[37][40]. Furthermore, the downstream melanocortin pathway appears to be more robustly engaged by POMC agonism than suppressed by NPY antagonism, suggesting that enhancing POMC (through enhanced satiety signaling) is preferable to blocking NPY.

**GIP Receptor Antagonism + GLP-1 Agonism:** While GIPR antagonism combined with GLP-1R agonism (as in the bispecific AMG 133) has shown promise in preclinical models, GIPR antagonism reduces GIP-stimulated insulin secretion and has unclear metabolic effects in humans[51][54]. Furthermore, clinical trials of tirzepatide (dual GLP-1/GIP agonist) suggest that GIP agonism, not antagonism, provides additional metabolic benefits[13][16][32]. Thus, GIPR antagonism is less preferred.

**Glucagon Receptor Agonism:** While glucagon agonism enhances thermogenesis and weight loss when combined with GLP-1R agonism (as in the triple agonist retatrutide), glucagon also causes nausea and hyperglycemia in some contexts[50][57][60]. Furthermore, the thermal benefits of glucagon are accompanied by hepatic glucose production, which could worsen glycemic control in some patients[50][53]. Glucagon is incorporated into next-generation multi-agonist therapies (tirzepatide, retatrutide) but represents a more complex safety profile than ActRII antagonism.

**Direct POMC Neuron Activation Agonists:** While POMC neuron activation promotes satiety and energy expenditure, developing an antibody agonist for this pathway is technically challenging. Melanocortin-4 receptor (MC4R) agonism is the downstream effector of POMC, and selective MC4R agonists are in development but are not antibody-based. Furthermore, MC4R agonists can cause adverse effects including hypertension and peripheral actions on pigmentation[27].

## Preclinical Validation Strategy and De-Risking Roadmap

### Proposed In Vitro and Preclinical In Vivo Studies

**ActRII Antagonist + GLP-1R Agonist Combination:**

1. **In Vitro Experiments:**
   - Co-culture of myoblasts with GLP-1R agonist ± ActRII antagonist; assess protein synthesis (via ³⁵S-methionine incorporation or Click-IT AHA), protein degradation (ubiquitin–proteasome activity), and myogenic marker expression (myogenin, myosin heavy chain)[18][19].
   - Assess ActRII antibody blocking activity and affinity in HEK293 cells expressing ActRII ± myostatin/activin A ligand competition assays.

2. **Preclinical In Vivo Studies (Diet-Induced Obesity Mouse Model):**
   - Groups: Placebo, GLP-1R agonist (semaglutide), ActRII antagonist (bimagrumab), GLP-1R agonist + ActRII antagonist.
   - Duration: 12–16 weeks of high-fat diet followed by 12–16 weeks of treatment.
   - Primary Endpoints:
     - Body weight and weight loss trajectory.
     - Lean mass (by EchoMRI or micro-CT), fat mass, and body composition changes (DXA equivalent).
     - Muscle mass and fiber cross-sectional area (CSA) by histology in tibialis anterior, soleus, and gastrocnemius.
     - Grip strength and treadmill endurance.
   - Secondary Endpoints:
     - Food intake and activity levels (via metabolic cages).
     - Glucose tolerance (glucose tolerance test, fasting insulin).
     - Indirect calorimetry (resting metabolic rate, energy expenditure).
     - Muscle protein synthesis rates (via ³H-phenylalanine incorporation or SUnSET assay).
     - Circulating markers: myostatin, activin A, IGF-1, growth hormone, testosterone.
     - Liver histology and lipid content.
     - Bone density and bone turnover markers (CTX, P1NP).
   - Safety Labs: Liver enzymes (ALT, AST), kidney function (creatinine, BUN), hemoglobin/hematocrit, lipid panel, heart rate, blood pressure.

**Oxytocin-Enhanced Satiety + GLP-1R Agonist Combination:**

1. **In Vitro Experiments:**
   - Assess oxytocin receptor agonist potency and selectivity in CHO cells expressing human oxytocin receptor (hOXTR) or vasopressin V1a receptor (off-target); measure cAMP production via HTRF or IP3 mobilization.
   - Blood–brain barrier penetration assays (if developing an antibody-based approach): assess transepithelial transport in in vitro BBB models (bEnd.3 cells or primary mouse brain endothelial cells).

2. **Preclinical In Vivo Studies (Diet-Induced Obesity Mice + Nausea Surrogate Model):**
   - Groups: Placebo, GLP-1R agonist (semaglutide), intranasal oxytocin or oxytocin receptor agonist peptide, GLP-1R agonist + oxytocin agonist.
   - Duration: 12–16 weeks of high-fat diet followed by 12–16 weeks of treatment.
   - Primary Endpoints:
     - Body weight and weight loss.
     - Food intake and activity.
     - Nausea Surrogate (Pica Behavior): Incidence and magnitude of kaolin (clay) intake, which is increased in rodent models of nausea and reduced by antiemetic agents[1]. Quantify kaolin consumption (g/day) as a measure of GLP-1–induced nausea.
     - Gastric Emptying: Quantify gastric clearance of a liquid test meal via scintigraphy or fluorescently-labeled meal tracking; compare GLP-1R agonist alone vs. combination with oxytocin agonist.
   - Secondary Endpoints:
     - Glucose tolerance, insulin sensitivity, lipid panel.
     - Indirect calorimetry (resting and total energy expenditure).
     - Cardiovascular parameters (heart rate, blood pressure, ECG if possible).
     - Brain oxytocin and oxytocinergic pathway markers (oxytocin receptor expression in hypothalamus, PVN, NTS via immunohistochemistry or quantitative PCR).
     - Behavior: Assess anxiety-like behavior (elevated plus maze, light-dark box) to evaluate potential neuropsychiatric effects.
   - Safety Labs: Same as ActRII studies, plus hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis markers (plasma corticosterone, adrenal weight).

### Go/No-Go Criteria for Advancement to IND-Enabling Studies

**ActRII Antagonist Combination:**
- **Go Criteria:** (1) Significantly greater lean mass preservation in the combination group vs. GLP-1R agonist alone (≥30% less lean mass loss). (2) No adverse effects on bone density or liver function. (3) Maintained glucose tolerance improvements. (4) No unexpected adverse events (behavioral, cardiovascular, immune).
- **No-Go Criteria:** (1) Significant bone loss exceeding that observed with GLP-1R agonist alone. (2) Liver enzyme elevation >3× upper limit of normal. (3) Unexpected bone fragility or fractures in animal models.

**Oxytocin Agonist Combination:**
- **Go Criteria:** (1) Significantly reduced kaolin intake (nausea surrogate) in combination group vs. GLP-1R agonist alone (≥40% reduction). (2) Maintained body weight loss efficacy. (3) No adverse effects on reproductive tissues (uterine weight, mammary hypertrophy) or cardiovascular parameters. (4) Acceptable tolerability and no unexpected neuropsychiatric effects.
- **No-Go Criteria:** (1) Exacerbation of nausea-surrogate behavior. (2) Significant reproductive or cardiovascular toxicity. (3) Behavioral abnormalities (excessive self-grooming, altered anxiety, aggression). (4) Tachyphylaxis (rapid loss of efficacy) in appetite suppression over 12–16 week treatment period.

## Clinical Trial Design and Regulatory Pathway for Next-Generation Combination Therapeutics

### Phase 2b Proof-of-Concept Trial Design: GLP-1R Agonist + ActRII Antagonist

**Primary Objective:** To evaluate whether ActRII antagonism combined with GLP-1R agonism improves body composition (greater fat loss, preserved lean mass) compared to GLP-1R agonist monotherapy in adults with obesity.

**Secondary Objectives:** To assess metabolic health improvements (glucose tolerance, lipid profile, insulin sensitivity), physical function (grip strength, 6-minute walk test), and safety/tolerability.

**Study Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group trial.

**Population:** Adults (age 18–75) with BMI 30–50 kg/m² and stable body weight for ≥3 months prior to enrollment. Exclusion: significant renal disease (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²), uncontrolled diabetes, cardiovascular disease, history of pancreatitis, pregnancy/lactation, malignancy.

**Treatment Groups:**
1. GLP-1R agonist (semaglutide, 0.5–1.0 mg subcutaneously weekly after 16-week titration) + placebo (IV infusion).
2. GLP-1R agonist (semaglutide) + ActRII antagonist (bimagrumab, 10 mg/kg IV every 4 weeks).
3. GLP-1R agonist + ActRII antagonist (18 mg/kg IV every 4 weeks, if 10 mg/kg is well-tolerated).
4. Placebo GLP-1R agonist (saline SC) + placebo ActRII antagonist (IV infusion).

**Duration:** 52 weeks of treatment (following 4-week run-in and 4-week screening periods).

**Primary Endpoints (52 weeks):**
- Change in total lean mass (kg) assessed by DXA; non-inferiority margin of 1 kg.
- Proportion of weight loss attributable to fat mass vs. lean mass.
- Change in fat mass (kg) assessed by DXA.

**Secondary Endpoints:**
- Absolute body weight change and percent body weight loss.
- Change in waist circumference and visceral adipose tissue (assessed by CT or MRI in a subset).
- Change in HOMA-IR, fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c (if baseline HbA1c >5.7%).
- Lipid panel changes (total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides).
- Change in strength (grip strength, leg press 1-rep max) and physical function (6-minute walk test, Timed Up and Go).
- Muscle quality (assessed by MRI-based measurement of intramuscular fat infiltration in a subset).
- Quality of life (IWQOL-Lite, SF-36).
- Incidence and severity of adverse events (particularly nausea, vomiting, gastrointestinal distress).

**Safety Endpoints:**
- Liver function tests (AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin), kidney function (creatinine, eGFR, BUN), hemoglobin/hematocrit.
- Bone turnover markers (P1NP, CTX) and BMD via DXA (hip, lumbar spine).
- Fasting lipid panel, including triglycerides and apolipoprotein levels.
- Heart rate, blood pressure, and 12-lead ECG at baseline and end-of-treatment.
- Immunogenicity (anti-bimagrumab antibodies).

## Summary and Conclusions

The clinical limitations of GLP-1R agonists in obesity treatment—including gastrointestinal adverse effects, lean muscle mass loss, and incomplete durability due to compensatory metabolic adaptation—reflect both the mechanistic constraints of targeting a single pathway and the body's robust counter-regulatory responses to sustained weight loss. A comprehensive analysis of the mechanistic limitations and compensatory pathways reveals multiple actionable nodes that are orthogonal to GLP-1 signaling and that could synergize with GLP-1R agonism to enhance metabolic benefit while mitigating adverse effects.

Two novel antibody-based therapeutic targets are proposed:

**1. Activin Type II Receptor (ActRII) Antagonism** directly addresses lean muscle mass loss by blocking the myostatin and activin A pathways that drive muscle catabolism during caloric deficit. Preclinical evidence demonstrates that ActRII antagonism combined with GLP-1R agonism achieves superior body composition improvements, with preserved lean mass despite significant fat loss. An IgG neutralizing antibody directed at ActRII (such as bimagrumab or garetosmab) combined with a GLP-1R agonist represents a clinically feasible and mechanistically rational next-generation therapy. The expected benefits include improved metabolic health (through preserved muscle mass and insulin sensitivity), enhanced physical function, and potentially reduced metabolic adaptation. Key safety considerations include monitoring of bone turnover and lipid parameters.

**2. Oxytocin Receptor-Enhanced Satiety Signaling** addresses GLP-1R agonist-induced nausea and enhances appetite suppression through a mechanism mechanistically distinct from GLP-1R activation in the gastrointestinal tract. Oxytocin signaling in the hypothalamus and brainstem promotes satiety and energy expenditure through pathways that converge with, but are distinct from, GLP-1R signaling. An intranasal oxytocin formulation (leveraging established clinical experience) combined with GLP-1R agonism, or a future bispecific antibody with GLP-1R agonism and oxytocin receptor agonism, could reduce nausea incidence while enhancing appetite suppression. The expected benefits include improved tolerability (reduced nausea), potentially enhanced and more sustained appetite suppression, and improved energy expenditure. Key safety considerations include monitoring for reproductive system effects and assessment of potential neuropsychiatric impacts with chronic oxytocin administration.

Together, these two targets provide mechanistically complementary benefits: ActRII antagonism addresses the metabolic/body composition limitations of GLP-1R agonism, while enhanced oxytocin signaling addresses the tolerability and appetite-suppression limitations. Preclinical validation studies in diet-induced obesity mouse models, followed by carefully designed Phase 2b proof-of-concept clinical trials, will establish the clinical utility and safety profile of these combination approaches. If successful, these next-generation combination therapeutics have the potential to represent a major advance in obesity treatment, achieving superior weight loss with improved body composition, enhanced tolerability, and potentially more durable metabolic benefits compared to GLP-1R monotherapy alone.

   - Weight loss under GLP-1R agonism can include **clinically meaningful lean mass loss**, with **high inter-individual variability** (driven by baseline sarcopenia risk, protein intake, resistance activity, age/sex hormones, and rate of weight loss).
   - This matters because skeletal muscle supports **function**, **resting metabolic rate**, and **insulin sensitivity**; disproportionate lean loss can blunt the “metabolic health” upside of weight reduction.

3. **Limited durability when therapy is reduced or discontinued**
   - **Weight regain** is common after stopping GLP-1R agonists, consistent with persistent biological pressure to restore defended weight.
   - This highlights that GLP-1R agonists often **manage** appetite rather than permanently reset the defended setpoint.

4. **Mechanistic imbalance: predominant intake suppression with incomplete coverage of energy-expenditure adaptation**
   - GLP-1–based therapies are primarily **intake-dominant**.
   - Compensatory reductions in energy expenditure (“metabolic adaptation”) and increased hunger signals can produce **plateaus** and increase the probability of regain unless additional nodes are targeted.

2) Proposal of Next-Generation Antibody Targets and Therapeutic Strategies

## Target 1: Myostatin (GDF8) neutralization to preserve lean mass during GLP-1–induced weight loss

**Why this target (biological function + pathway context)**
- **Myostatin (GDF8)** is a secreted negative regulator of skeletal muscle mass. In negative energy balance, increased catabolic pressure can unmask or amplify the functional consequences of inhibitory muscle pathways.
- This target is selected to directly address a core limitation of GLP-1–based obesity pharmacotherapy: **loss of lean mass and functional capacity** during weight reduction.

**How it complements GLP-1**
- GLP-1R agonists primarily reduce **energy intake**; myostatin neutralization acts orthogonally by improving **body-composition quality** (fat loss with less lean loss), rather than attempting to further intensify anorexia (which can worsen GI tolerability).

**Antibody modality**
- **Neutralizing anti-myostatin monoclonal antibody (IgG)** (ligand trap by antibody binding), optimized for durable peripheral exposure.
- Rationale for ligand (myostatin) targeting versus broad receptor blockade: improves focus on the muscle constraint node while potentially reducing broader pleiotropic liabilities associated with less selective pathway blockade. (Related pathway programs such as **ActRII inhibitors, including bimagrumab**, highlight both promise and the importance of careful endpoint selection and safety monitoring.)

**Therapeutic strategy**
- **Combination therapy:** GLP-1R agonist (standard-of-care titration) **+ anti-myostatin IgG**.
- **Timing (explicitly treated as a testable assumption, not a fixed rule):**
  - Two rational schedules should be evaluated:
    1) **Early-start** (co-initiation or near initiation): maximize prevention of early lean loss during rapid weight reduction.
    2) **Step-in** after GLP-1 tolerability titration: reduce confounding of early GI symptoms and simplify attribution of adverse events.
  - The optimal schedule is empirical and should be resolved by body-composition and function endpoints.

**Expected improvements over GLP-1 monotherapy**
- **Improved body-composition quality:** reduced lean mass loss for a given total weight reduction.
- **Functional preservation:** better maintenance of strength/physical performance during weight loss.
- **Metabolic health support:** preserved muscle mass may support insulin sensitivity and mitigate declines in resting energy expenditure that accompany lean mass loss (directionally expected; requires confirmation).

**Potential risks, trade-offs, and safety considerations (expanded)**
- **Musculoskeletal risks:** disproportionate hypertrophy or altered muscle–tendon balance could theoretically increase injury risk; requires monitoring of functional outcomes, tendon/joint symptoms, and activity guidance.
- **Off-target or systemic effects:** while myostatin is more muscle-focused than broad receptor blockade, long-term systemic consequences remain a development consideration.
- **Class-context caution from related pathway inhibitors:** prior ActRII-pathway clinical experience (e.g., programs such as bimagrumab) motivates heightened vigilance for **vascular/mucocutaneous signals (e.g., epistaxis, telangiectasia-like findings)** and other unanticipated pleiotropic effects; even if myostatin targeting is intended to be narrower, safety surveillance should explicitly include these domains.
- **Clinical positioning trade-off:** this target is not designed to directly reduce nausea; its value proposition is **metabolic quality and function preservation**, enabling healthier weight loss.

**Patient stratification / biomarkers (development-enabling, not new experiments)**
- Enrich for those at higher lean-loss risk: low baseline lean mass index, older age, low resistance activity, low protein intake, frailty signals.
- Track: DXA/MRI body composition, strength (e.g., grip/leg extension), gait/performance measures, creatinine/cystatin C as supportive muscle-mass correlates, and patient-reported function.

## Target 2: Ghrelin neutralization to blunt compensatory hunger and improve durability (with potential GLP-1 dose-sparing for GI tolerability)

**Why this target (biological function + pathway context)**
- **Ghrelin** is a circulating orexigenic hormone that promotes hunger, meal initiation, and food-seeking behavior. In weight-reduced states, ghrelin-driven hunger can contribute to **plateaus and regain**, a central limitation of GLP-1–based approaches once patients adapt or discontinue therapy.
- Because ghrelin is a **soluble peripheral ligand**, it is a highly actionable antibody target compared with CNS-delivery-dependent concepts.

**How it complements GLP-1**
- GLP-1R agonists provide strong satiety signaling, but compensatory orexigenic drivers can persist. Neutralizing ghrelin aims to reduce the **counter-regulatory “push back”** toward increased intake.
- **GI tolerability linkage (carefully qualified):** if ghrelin neutralization reduces hunger and food drive, it may allow achieving desired appetite control with **lower GLP-1 exposure** in some patients, which could plausibly reduce GI adverse effects. This is a mechanistic hypothesis and must be validated with dose–tolerability endpoints.

**Antibody modality**
- **Neutralizing anti-ghrelin monoclonal antibody (IgG)** (designed to bind circulating ghrelin and reduce receptor activation).
- Optional engineering consideration within standard antibody scope: Fc optimization for half-life to support infrequent dosing.

**Therapeutic strategy**
- **Combination therapy:** GLP-1R agonist **+ anti-ghrelin IgG**.
- Development approach consistent with the rationale:
  - Evaluate whether anti-ghrelin enables **GLP-1 dose de-escalation** while preserving weight-loss efficacy and improving tolerability.
  - Evaluate durability endpoints (maintenance phase) where ghrelin-driven hunger is expected to be most relevant.

**Expected improvements over GLP-1 monotherapy**
- **Durability/regain mitigation:** reduced compensatory hunger signaling that can drive rebound eating when weight decreases or when GLP-1 dose is lowered.
