## (1) Possible molecular mechanisms by which the viral CRE increases RNA stability, and validation strategies

### Hypothesis 1: CRE‑dependent recruitment of stabilizing RNA‑binding proteins (RBPs)
**Mechanism**  
The viral CRE located in the 3′ UTR serves as a binding platform for host RBPs (e.g., PABPC1, ELAVL1/HuR). This promotes mRNA circularization through enhanced PABPC–eIF4G interaction, sterically shielding the poly(A) tail from CCR4–NOT‑mediated deadenylation.

**Validation**
- CLIP‑seq or RIP‑qPCR for PABPC1 and ELAVL1 comparing CRE vs. control RNAs  
- CRE mutational analysis followed by actinomycin‑D chase to quantify half‑life  
- Polysome profiling to assess coupling of stabilization and translation

---

### Hypothesis 2: CRE‑mediated poly(A) tail remodeling with non‑A nucleotides
**Mechanism**  
The CRE recruits non‑canonical poly(A) polymerases (e.g., PAPD5/TENT4B, TENT5A), generating mixed poly(A) tails containing intermittent G or U residues. Such heterotypic tails are less efficiently recognized by deadenylases, prolonging mRNA lifespan.

**Validation**
- Nanopore dRNA‑seq–based tail composition analysis (see Sections 3–4)  
- Knockdown of PAPD5/TENT5A followed by rescue with catalytic mutants  
- In vitro tail extension assays using CRE‑containing RNA substrates

---

### Hypothesis 3: CRE‑dependent inhibition of deadenylase recruitment
**Mechanism**  
The CRE indirectly blocks CCR4–NOT or PAN2–PAN3 access by recruiting inhibitory RBPs or inducing a local RNA structure unfavorable for deadenylase engagement, slowing tail shortening without necessarily increasing initial tail length.

**Validation**
- Deadenylation time‑course assays in cytoplasmic extracts  
- CLIP‑seq for CNOT6/7 in CRE vs. control contexts  
- RBP knockdown combined with tail‑length profiling

---

### Hypothesis 4: Controlled uridylation as a protective intermediate
**Mechanism**  
Low‑frequency, internally positioned uridylation events mediated by TUT4/7 may interfere with processive deadenylation without triggering rapid decay, contributing to stabilization in active cells.

**Validation**
- Comparative dRNA‑seq signal analysis for U‑associated deviations  
- TUT4/7 depletion followed by CRE‑dependent stability assays  
- Time‑resolved tail composition profiling

### Title
A Viral cis‑Regulatory Element Promotes Host‑Dependent Poly(A) Tail Remodeling to Stabilize mRNA

### Abstract
RNA viruses frequently manipulate host RNA metabolism to ensure transcript persistence. Here, we investigate a viral cis‑regulatory element (CRE) that confers a >10‑fold increase in mRNA expression when inserted into the 3′ UTR of a reporter transcript. Using nanopore direct RNA sequencing, we show that CRE‑dependent stabilization correlates with dramatic poly(A) tail elongation and the appearance of internal tail modifications, but only in a subset of host cells competent for this response. Inactive cells fail to exhibit both stabilization and tail remodeling, indicating a strict dependence on host enzymatic context. Signal‑level analysis reveals clustered deviations within poly(A) tails, consistent with mixed‑composition tails generated by non‑canonical poly(A) polymerases. We propose a model in which the CRE recruits host factors such as PAPD5/TENT5A and stabilizing RBPs, leading to tail remodeling that impairs deadenylation. These findings reveal a host‑conditional mechanism of viral RNA stabilization and suggest strategies for engineering long‑lived therapeutic mRNAs.

### Introduction
Messenger RNA (mRNA) stability is a critical determinant of gene expression output in eukaryotic cells. Following transcription and processing, mRNA lifetimes are shaped by coordinated interactions between cis‑acting RNA elements and trans‑acting host factors that govern deadenylation, decapping, and exonucleolytic decay. Viruses have evolved sophisticated strategies to exploit these pathways, both by accelerating decay of host transcripts and by protecting their own RNAs from degradation. Viral cis‑regulatory elements (CREs) embedded within untranslated regions are increasingly recognized as key determinants of viral RNA fate, yet the molecular principles governing their function remain incompletely understood.

In this study, we investigate a CRE derived from an RNA virus that, when inserted into the 3′ UTR of an otherwise identical reporter mRNA, confers a striking increase in protein expression and RNA stability. Using lipid nanoparticle–mediated delivery of in vitro–transcribed RNAs, we observe a >10‑fold enhancement of reporter expression at 24 hours post‑transfection relative to CRE‑lacking controls. Importantly, this stabilization phenotype is observed only in a subset of host cells (“active”), whereas others (“inactive”) fail to respond despite carrying the same CRE sequence. This dichotomy provides a unique opportunity to dissect host‑dependent mechanisms of CRE function.

Nanopore direct RNA sequencing (dRNA‑seq) enables simultaneous interrogation of RNA sequence, poly(A) tail length, and signal‑level features that may reflect base composition or chemical modifications. Leveraging this technology, we analyze poly(A) tail dynamics and signal perturbations across active and inactive conditions, revealing CRE‑dependent tail elongation and internal signal deviations consistent with tail remodeling. Together, these data support a model in which the viral CRE recruits host poly(A)–modifying enzymes in a context‑dependent manner to stabilize RNA.

### Results
**CRE‑dependent stabilization is conditional on host cell state.** Reporter RNAs containing the viral CRE exhibited robust stabilization and elevated protein output in active cells but not in inactive cells. This dichotomy was reproducible across biological replicates and could not be attributed to differences in RNA delivery or initial translation efficiency, indicating a post‑transcriptional mechanism dependent on host factors.

**CRE induces poly(A) tail elongation in active cells.** dRNA‑seq–based poly(A) tail estimation revealed a pronounced rightward shift in tail length distributions for active‑CRE RNAs, with median lengths approximately doubled relative to controls. In contrast, inactive‑CRE RNAs exhibited tail lengths indistinguishable from inactive controls, implicating a failure of tail remodeling in the inactive state.

**Internal signal deviations suggest tail remodeling.** Analysis of raw nanopore current signals identified reproducible internal deviations within poly(A) tails of active‑CRE RNAs. These deviations were clustered at defined positions relative to the body–tail junction and were rare in all other conditions. Given known limitations of homopolymer sequencing, these patterns were interpreted conservatively as regional tail perturbations rather than definitive base calls.

**Comparative analysis links tail remodeling to stabilization.** Quantification of reads exhibiting internal deviations demonstrated strong enrichment in active‑CRE samples. The concordance between tail elongation, signal deviations, and stabilization supports a unified mechanism in which tail remodeling underlies RNA persistence.

**Mechanistic model and candidate enzymes.** Integrating these observations, we propose that the CRE recruits non‑canonical poly(A) polymerases such as PAPD5 and TENT5A, possibly in concert with stabilizing RBPs, to generate elongated and compositionally heterogeneous poly(A) tails that resist deadenylation.

### Discussion
Our findings support a model in which a viral CRE promotes RNA stability by engaging host poly(A) metabolism rather than merely blocking decay. The strict dependence on host cell state explains why identical CREs can be functional in some contexts but inert in others. The inferred involvement of non‑canonical poly(A) polymerases aligns with emerging paradigms of mixed‑tail synthesis as a regulatory mechanism.

Importantly, while nanopore dRNA‑seq provides unique access to tail‑level information, homopolymer analysis remains technically challenging. Thus, our conclusions regarding tail composition are necessarily inferential and will require orthogonal validation. Nonetheless, the strong correlation between tail elongation, signal perturbation, and functional stabilization argues that tail remodeling is central to CRE activity.

Beyond virology, these insights have practical implications for the design of stabilized therapeutic mRNAs. Incorporation of CRE‑like elements could enable host‑assisted enhancement of RNA persistence, improving efficacy while minimizing chemical modification.

    The interpretation of the poly(A) tail length data (Active-CRE ~150 nt vs. Active-Control ~75 nt) implies active elongation. However, the report does not define the **initial poly(A) length of the IVT mRNA** (T0).  
    - If T0 was ~150 nt: The Control underwent deadenylation (decay), and CRE merely protected it.

    Without stating the T0 assumption, the discrimination between "protection" (Hypothesis 3) and "elongation" (Hypothesis 2) is ambiguous, though the report favors elongation later.

3.  **Overestimation of Nanopore Homopolymer Sensitivity**:  
    In Sections (3) and (4), the report relies heavily on identifying "clustered internal signal deviations" within poly(A) tails. In actual Nanopore dRNA-seq, resolving internal modifications within long homopolymers is technically fraught with high error rates and signal compression/slippage. Standard basecalling/alignment pipelines (like those implied by `.bam` usage) are often insufficient to distinguish modified bases from random dwell-time noise in homopolymers without training on synthetic controls. The report treats this analysis as more definitive than current standard technology typically allows.
    
4.  **Disconnect Between Hypothesis 3 and Simulated Data**:  
    Hypothesis 3 suggests "inhibition of deadenylase recruitment." While a valid stabilization mechanism, it conflicts with the simulated result of gross tail elongation (up to 150 nt). If Hypothesis 3 were the sole mechanism, tail lengths should effectively stagnate or shorten very slowly, not increase significantly beyond the control/IVT length. The Discussion should explicitly rule out Hypothesis 3 based on the length data, which it fails to do clearly.

## Final Short Summary to Attach
The report provides a scientifically coherent and logically sound analysis of a hypothetical RNA stabilization scenario, correctly linking CRE function to cytoplasmic polyadenylation and possible non-canonical tailing. However, it significantly underdelivers on the required length for the manuscript draft and relies on optimistic assumptions regarding the resolution of Nanopore sequencing for internal homopolymer modifications. A rigorous review would demand experimental validation of the modification calling method and a clear definition of the input RNA tail length to distinguish between elongation and protection.
