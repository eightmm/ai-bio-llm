# Molecular Mechanisms of Viral cis-Regulatory Element–Mediated mRNA Stabilization: A Comprehensive Analysis of Poly(A) Tail Dynamics and Host Enzyme Engagement in Therapeutic RNA Design

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive research report addresses the molecular mechanisms by which a viral cis-regulatory element enhances mRNA stability in human cells, with particular emphasis on poly(A) tail metabolism, RNA modification dynamics detected by nanopore direct RNA sequencing, identification of responsible host enzymes, and integration of findings into a publication-ready manuscript framework. The analysis synthesizes prior literature on mRNA decay pathways, poly(A) tail regulation, and viral mechanisms of host takeover to propose prioritized hypotheses and experimental strategies. Through systematic evaluation of multiple stabilization mechanisms, enzyme prioritization frameworks, and downstream characterization strategies, this report establishes a comprehensive research roadmap for mechanistic dissection and therapeutic exploitation of RNA stabilization principles.

## Section 1: Mechanistic Hypotheses for Viral CRE-Mediated RNA Stabilization

### 1.1 Introduction to mRNA Stability as a Central Regulatory Node

The stability of messenger RNA represents one of the most critical control points in eukaryotic gene expression, with mRNA half-lives ranging from minutes to hours depending on cellular context and sequence features[1][4]. The poly(A) tail serves as a primary determinant of mRNA stability, functioning both as a binding substrate for protective proteins and as a regulated entry point for degradation machinery[7][25][40]. Extensive research has established that deadenylation—the shortening of the poly(A) tail—is the rate-limiting step in cytoplasmic mRNA decay, initiating a cascade that leads to either 5'-to-3' exonucleolytic degradation via decapping and Xrn1, or 3'-to-5' exonuclease activity via the exosome[6][20][37].

Viral pathogens have evolved sophisticated strategies to manipulate host mRNA metabolism, either to suppress antiviral gene expression through targeted host shutoff, or to protect their own genomic and transcribed RNAs from premature degradation[15][18]. The identification of a viral-derived cis-regulatory element that enhances host mRNA stability suggests a pathogen-derived "anti-degradation" mechanism that could involve multiple molecular pathways.

### 1.2 Hypothesis 1: Enhanced Poly(A) Binding Protein Recruitment and Circularization

**Mechanistic Basis:** One fundamental mRNA stabilization mechanism involves increased recruitment and stabilization of poly(A) binding proteins, particularly cytoplasmic poly(A) binding protein (PABPC). The poly(A) tail and its associated PABPC proteins form a ribonucleoprotein complex that protects the 3' end from exonucleolytic attack[7]. Additionally, circularization of mRNA through interaction between PABPC and the 5'-cap-binding complex (eIF4E–eIF4G–PABPC) creates a closed-loop structure that enhances both translation and stability[10]. Some viral RNA-binding proteins exploit this mechanism by directly recruiting PABPC in a poly(A) tail-independent manner[10].

**Proposed Mechanism:** The viral CRE could encode a protein-binding motif(s) for AU-rich element binding proteins (AREBPs) that promote stabilizing complex formation. Alternatively, the CRE might present a secondary structure recognized by trans-acting factors that enhance PABPC binding or oligomerization, thereby increasing protection against deadenylases.

**Validation Strategies:**

1. **CLIP-seq and RIP-seq Analysis:** Perform crosslinking immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing[33] to identify which host proteins preferentially bind to CRE-containing transcripts. Use both general RNA-binding proteins and pathway-specific PABPC and PABPN1 antibodies to map interaction sites within the CRE region. Expected outcome: CRE-containing mRNAs should show enriched PABPC and stabilizing AREBP binding compared to control constructs.

2. **RNA Circularization Assays:** Employ circLigase or proximity ligation assay (PLA) to directly measure whether CRE expression promotes formation of closed-loop mRNA structures[10]. A positive result would show increased interaction between 5' cap-binding proteins and 3' PABPC-containing ribonucleoproteins in active versus inactive cells.

3. **Mutagenesis and Deletion Analysis:** Systematically delete or mutate putative protein-binding motifs within the CRE and assay remaining mRNA stability, half-life, and associated protein binding. Progressive loss of stabilization correlating with disrupted protein recognition would support this hypothesis.

4. **In Vitro Reconstitution:** Perform binding assays using recombinant PABPC, eIF4E, eIF4G, and cell extracts with synthetic CRE-containing RNA constructs to demonstrate direct enhancement of circularization complex formation.

### 1.3 Hypothesis 2: Suppression of Deadenylation through Non-A Nucleotide Incorporation and Altered Poly(A) Tail Composition

**Mechanistic Basis:** Recent evidence demonstrates that viral and host polyadenylating enzymes can incorporate non-adenine nucleotides (guanosines, uridines, cytosines) into mRNA poly(A) tails, a process that substantially alters tail degradation kinetics[3][49][52]. "Mixed-tail" poly(A) sequences containing intermittent non-A residues are protected from rapid deadenylation by the major cytoplasmic deadenylases CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3[49]. This mechanism has been documented in hepatitis B virus and coronavirus-infected cells, where specific poly(A) polymerases and terminal nucleotidyl transferases are upregulated or recruited to create stabilized poly(A) tails[49][52].

**Proposed Mechanism:** The viral CRE could recruit host poly(A) polymerases (particularly the non-canonical members PAPD5, PAPD7, TENT5A, or TUT enzymes) to the mRNA 3' end, promoting incorporation of non-A nucleotides that slow or block deadenylation. The CRE might serve as a recognition site for an RNA-binding protein that recruits these enzymes, or the CRE structure itself might be directly recognized by the polymerase recruitment machinery.

**Validation Strategies:**

1. **Nanopore dRNA-seq Quantification of Tail Composition:** Use tailfindr, nanopolish polya, or Dorado-based analysis tools to measure poly(A) tail length and estimate non-A nucleotide frequency in CRE-containing versus control mRNAs[2][5][56][59]. Expected outcome: active-cre samples should show higher average tail length and elevated non-A nucleotide incorporation compared to active-control and inactive samples.

2. **Direct Measurement of Deadenylation Kinetics:** Perform pulse-chase or transcriptional pulsing experiments combined with quantitative northern blotting or qRT-PCR to directly measure the rate of poly(A) shortening in cells expressing CRE versus control constructs. Slower deadenylation in CRE-expressing cells would support this hypothesis[46][54].

3. **Targeted Knockdown of Candidate Poly(A) Polymerases:** Use CRISPR-Cas9 or siRNA to independently deplete PAPD5, PAPD7, TENT5A, TUT1, TUT4, and TUT7[14][17][19][52]. Predict that knockdown of the responsible enzyme would substantially reduce the protective effect of the CRE, while knockdown of uninvolved enzymes would have minimal impact.

4. **In Vitro Polyadenylation Assays:** Incubate synthetic CRE-containing RNA constructs with purified or cell-derived poly(A) polymerase in the presence of ATP, GTP, or other nucleotides, measuring incorporation rates of non-A residues via LC-MS/MS or high-resolution mass spectrometry.

5. **Crystal Structure and Modeling:** If a candidate polymerase is identified, determine whether it specifically recognizes CRE sequence motifs through structural analysis, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA), or AlphaFold-based structure prediction.

### 1.4 Hypothesis 3: RNA Modification-Mediated Stabilization through Uridylation and Uridylation-Dependent Decay Inhibition

**Mechanistic Basis:** Terminal uridylation of mRNA 3' ends has emerged as a critical regulatory axis controlling the balance between 5'-to-3' and 3'-to-5' mRNA decay pathways[3]. In fission yeast and mammalian cells, terminal nucleotidyl transferase Cid1 and related human enzymes (including TENT1 and TUT enzymes) catalyze mono- or oligo-uridylation of poly(A) tails or completely deadenylated substrates. This uridylation serves dual and apparently contradictory roles: on mRNAs with residual poly(A) tails, uridylation facilitates binding of the Lsm1–7 complex, promoting decapping and 5'-to-3' decay. However, excessive deadenylation followed by oligouridylation can trigger 3'-to-5' degradation via the Dis3l2 exonuclease[3]. Critically, uridylation also protects mRNAs from excessive deadenylation, preventing progression to the terminal oligouridylation step that initiates exosomal decay.

**Proposed Mechanism:** The viral CRE could promote selective uridylation of poly(A) tails at intermediate tail lengths (e.g., 50–100 nt), without allowing progression to full deadenylation and subsequent oligouridylation. This "intermediate uridylation" could stabilize transcripts by (1) preventing complete deadenylation-dependent decay commitment, (2) recruiting protective factors rather than decay factors, or (3) altering mRNA secondary structure to reduce accessibility to decay machinery.

**Validation Strategies:**

1. **Selective Analysis of Uridine Composition in Nanopore Data:** Use current signal analysis (Tombo or other modification-detection algorithms)[16][55][58] to identify position-specific uridylation patterns within poly(A) regions of CRE-containing transcripts. Expected outcome: active-cre mRNAs should show enriched uridine incorporation at specific tail lengths compared to control.

2. **Temporal Uridylation Mapping:** Perform time-course dRNA-seq experiments, collecting samples at multiple timepoints (e.g., 0, 6, 12, 24 hours) post-transfection. Plot uridylation patterns as a function of poly(A) tail length and time, predicting that CRE-containing transcripts maintain an intermediate uridylation state while control mRNAs progress toward complete deadenylation or oligouridylation.

3. **Targeted TUTase Knockdown:** Deplete TUT1, TUT4, TUT7, or TENT1/4/5 enzymes and measure effects on CRE-dependent stabilization. Predict selective rescue or reduction depending on which TUTase(s) mediate the stabilizing uridylation.

4. **Lsm1–7 Complex Localization:** Use immunofluorescence or proximity ligation assay to assess whether CRE-containing transcripts preferentially recruit or avoid Lsm1–7 complex. If uridylation protects without triggering decay-promoting complex formation, CRE mRNAs should show reduced Lsm1–7 association compared to heavily uridylated control transcripts.

### 1.5 Hypothesis 4: Direct Inhibition of CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3 Deadenylase Complexes

**Mechanistic Basis:** The CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3 deadenylase complexes are responsible for nearly all cytoplasmic deadenylation in eukaryotic cells[20][23][37][40]. These complexes can be inhibited or their activity redirected through multiple mechanisms, including recruitment of cofactors, sequestration by competing RNA-binding proteins, or post-translational modification. For example, phosphorylation of the zinc-finger protein TTP modulates its recruitment of CCR4–NOT, thereby controlling whether ARE-containing mRNAs undergo decay or stabilization[38].

**Proposed Mechanism:** The viral CRE could encode a binding site for an RNA-binding protein that competes with deadenylase recruitment factors, or the CRE could recruit a cofactor that inhibits deadenylase catalytic activity. Alternatively, the CRE might promote binding of stabilizing proteins (such as HuR or related ELAVL family members) that occlude deadenylase access to the poly(A) tract.

**Validation Strategies:**

1. **Co-immunoprecipitation and In Vitro Deadenylase Assays:** Incubate cell extracts or purified CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3 complexes with CRE-containing versus control RNA substrates, measuring deadenylation kinetics. If deadenylation is inhibited on CRE substrates, conduct further experiments to identify which subunits or factors mediate inhibition.

2. **RNA-Binding Protein Screen:** Use RIP-seq or CLIP-seq with a panel of antibodies against known deadenylase regulators (GW182, TNRC6 proteins, TTP, HuR, ELAVL proteins, etc.) to identify which proteins are preferentially associated with CRE-containing transcripts.

3. **Cellular Factor Depletion:** Systematically knock down candidate inhibitory factors and assess the rescue or failure of CRE-dependent stabilization. Expected outcome: knockdown of a critical inhibitory factor should reduce stabilization.

### 1.6 Hypothesis 5: Coupling Between Translation and Poly(A) Stability Through Ribosome-Mediated Effects

**Mechanistic Basis:** Ribosome translocation, ribosome occupancy, and specific features of the coding sequence substantially influence mRNA stability through a process termed "codon optimality-mediated decay" or "translation-dependent stability."[1] Highly structured coding sequences can reduce ribosome loading, and this reduced translational activity associates with altered mRNA stability. Additionally, some viral RNAs achieve stabilization through optimization of codon usage, ribosome binding sites, or RNA secondary structure in the coding region rather than through 3' UTR mechanisms.

**Proposed Mechanism:** The viral CRE might not directly affect poly(A) tail metabolism but could instead alter mRNA secondary structure or translation efficiency through long-range interactions, thereby indirectly enhancing stability by coupling to translation-dependent stabilization pathways.

**Validation Strategies:**

1. **Ribosome Profiling and Polysome Analysis:** Perform ribosome profiling (Ribo-seq) combined with polysome fractionation to measure translational efficiency and ribosome occupancy on CRE-containing versus control transcripts. Expected outcome: CRE-containing mRNAs should show either elevated ribosome occupancy associated with protection from decay machinery, or an altered codon optimality profile.

2. **Translation Inhibition Experiments:** Treat cells with translation inhibitors (cycloheximide, puromycin) and measure CRE-mediated stabilization in the presence or absence of active translation. If stabilization is translation-dependent, inhibitor treatment should eliminate the protective effect.

3. **RNA Secondary Structure Analysis:** Use SHAPE, DMS, or similar footprinting methods combined with nanopore signal analysis to assess whether the CRE promotes formation of secondary structures that affect ribosome accessibility or decay machinery engagement.

## Section 2: Integrated Nanopore Direct RNA Sequencing Analysis of Poly(A) Tail Dynamics

### 2.1 Methodological Framework for dRNA-seq Analysis

Nanopore direct RNA sequencing enables detection of individual mRNA molecules with intact poly(A) tails and associated chemical modifications, providing single-molecule resolution unobtainable through conventional RNA sequencing methods[2][5][16]. The raw ionic current data ("squiggle") generated during sequencing contains information about nucleotide identity, sequence context, and chemical modifications at nucleotide resolution. Modern analytical approaches employ multiple orthogonal strategies to extract poly(A) tail information: alignment-based methods (nanopolish polya, tailfindr) that rely on base-calling accuracy, and signal-level methods (Remora, Tombo, machine learning approaches) that detect modifications through deviations in current signal[55][56][58][59].

### 2.2 Expected Poly(A) Tail Length Distributions and Comparisons

Based on the experimental design and prior literature, we expect the following poly(A) tail length patterns:

**Active-Control versus Active-CRE Comparison:** In active (responsive) cells, CRE-containing mRNAs should exhibit longer median poly(A) tail lengths compared to control mRNAs at the 24-hour timepoint. This could reflect either reduced deadenylation kinetics (slowed initial decay phase) or de novo polyadenylation-mediated tail elongation by upregulated poly(A) polymerases. Expected difference: CRE transcripts could show 20–80 nucleotide median tail length advantage, depending on the intensity of the stabilization response[49].

**Active versus Inactive Cell Comparison:** Active cells (with apparent CRE response) should show markedly different poly(A) tail distributions across all conditions compared to inactive cells lacking the response phenotype. This could indicate that active cells have upregulated or recruited stabilization machinery (poly(A) polymerases, protective proteins), while inactive cells lack this capacity. Statistical comparison (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, Wilcoxon rank-sum test) should show significant p-values (p < 0.001) for active-cre versus inactive-cre comparisons.

### 2.3 Expected Non-A Nucleotide Incorporation and Modification Patterns

Based on Hypothesis 2 and recent viral infection studies[49][52], we predict the following patterns:

**Guanosine (G) Incorporation:** Active-cre samples should show elevated G-nucleotide incorporation at ~5–15% frequency within poly(A) regions, compared to <2% in active-control and inactive samples. This would align with PAPD5/7-mediated "guanylation" observed in HBV and viral infections. The distribution should be relatively uniform across poly(A) regions or slightly enriched toward the proximal tail (near the 3' mRNA body junction).

**Uridine (U) Incorporation:** Expected frequency of 2–8% in active-cre samples, potentially higher than controls. Uridine positioning might show clustered patterns (e.g., multiple consecutive Us at specific tail lengths), suggesting TUTase-catalyzed oligouridylation at intermediate steps of deadenylation.

**5-methylcytosine (m5C) and Other Modifications:** Detection of m5C, m6A, or pseudouridine modifications within poly(A) regions would be unexpected in poly(A) tails themselves (as these are adenine homopolymers) but could indicate read-through into downstream regions or labeling artifacts. Focus should remain on A-to-non-A substitutions.

### 2.5 Statistical Rigor and Interpretation

All comparisons should include:
1. **Sample size calculation and power analysis:** Report numbers of unique molecules sequenced per condition (target >5,000 reads per condition for sufficient statistical power).
2. **Multiple hypothesis correction:** Apply Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction across multiple comparisons.
3. **Effect size reporting:** Include Cohen's d, eta-squared, or similar metrics alongside p-values.
4. **Reproducibility:** Report biological replicates independently and assess inter-replicate correlation.

### 2.6 Biological Interpretation in the Context of Viral CRE Function

The poly(A) tail composition and length data should be interpreted as direct evidence for specific molecular mechanisms:

- **Elevated G incorporation coupled with longer tails in active-cre samples** strongly supports Hypothesis 2 (non-A nucleotide incorporation by viral-recruited poly(A) polymerases).
- **Uridine clustering at intermediate tail lengths** supports Hypothesis 3 (translation-independent uridylation-mediated protection).
- **Normal or only slightly elevated tail lengths but enriched in protective proteins** would favor Hypothesis 1 (PABPC recruitment).
- **Dramatic tail length differences uncorrelated with modified bases** would suggest Hypothesis 4 or 5 (deadenylase inhibition or translation-coupled effects).

## Section 3: Host Enzyme Prioritization for Poly(A)-Mediated Stabilization

### 3.1 Ranking Framework and Prioritization Criteria

Five human enzymes are prioritized based on: (1) known capacity to modify poly(A) tails or regulate deadenylation; (2) precedent in viral infection or host mRNA stabilization; (3) consistency with observed nanopore data patterns; (4) subcellular localization matching the expected site of CRE action; and (5) druggability and mechanistic clarity.

### 3.2 Priority Ranking and Supporting Evidence

**Rank 1: PAPD5 (Poly(A) Polymerase D5)**

*Mechanism:* PAPD5 is a non-canonical cytoplasmic poly(A) polymerase capable of extending poly(A) tails and incorporating non-A nucleotides (particularly guanosines)[52]. It functions as a "terminal nucleotidyl transferase" distinct from the canonical nuclear PAP. PAPD5 directly participates in stabilizing hepatitis B virus RNA by extending its poly(A) tail with mixed A/G composition, protecting against CCR4–NOT-mediated deadenylation[52].

*Supporting Evidence:* (1) PAPD5 knockout cells show reduced poly(A) tail length and G incorporation in viral RNAs[52]; (2) PAPD5 exhibits cytoplasmic localization consistent with mRNA 3' end modification; (3) PAPD5 catalytic activity is separable from that of PAPD7, suggesting specific substrate or localization preferences; (4) Inhibition of PAPD5 enzymatic activity significantly reduces viral RNA accumulation and stability.

*Data Interpretation Link:* If active-cre samples show elevated G-incorporation and longer tails, PAPD5 would be the top candidate. Predicted mechanism: CRE recruits PAPD5 (directly or via an adaptor protein), extending the poly(A) tail and incorporating Gs to create "mixed tail" resistant to deadenylation[49][52].

*Literature Evidence:* Recent studies demonstrate PAPD5-mediated G-tailing in HBV and other viral contexts, with PAPD5 antagonism conferring partial antiviral activity[52].

---

**Rank 2: TENT5A (Terminal Nucleotidyl Transferase 5A)**

*Mechanism:* TENT5A is a cytoplasmic non-canonical poly(A) polymerase highly induced by immunological stimuli and viral infection[49]. It functions as a TUTase capable of mono-, di-, or longer oligo-uridylation of mRNA 3' ends, and also exhibits some adenylylation activity to extend poly(A) tails.

*Supporting Evidence:* (1) TENT5A is upregulated in response to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) stimulation and promotes poly(A) tail extension of immune response transcripts[49]; (2) TENT5A was recently shown to mediate re-adenylation of therapeutic mRNA (mRNA-1273 vaccine) in macrophages, extending poly(A) tails from ~100 to ~200 nucleotides and enhancing protein expression and stability[49]; (3) TENT5A displays both adenylylation and uridylylation activities, providing flexibility for multiple stabilization modes; (4) TENT5A is directly inducible by viral PAMPs, consistent with being a host factor manipulated by viruses.

*Data Interpretation Link:* If active-cre samples show simultaneous tail elongation and uridine incorporation at intermediate positions, TENT5A would be highly relevant. TENT5A might represent a "stress-response" pathway activated specifically in response to viral CRE expression.

*Literature Evidence:* The recent discovery that TENT5A mediates vaccine mRNA stabilization through poly(A) extension represents a paradigm for how viral or viral-mimicking CREs could enhance mRNA lifespan[49].

---

**Rank 3: CCR4–NOT Complex (CNOT6 and CNOT7 Subunits)**

*Mechanism:* Although primarily known as the major deadenylase complex catalyzing mRNA decay, CCR4–NOT can be inhibited or redirected by RNA-binding proteins and post-translational modifications[20][23][38][40]. The CCR4–NOT complex is recruited to specific mRNAs through adaptor proteins (e.g., GW182 for miRNA-targeted transcripts, TTP for ARE-containing mRNAs), and this recruitment can be blocked or reduced by competing stabilizing factors.

*Supporting Evidence:* (1) Phosphorylation of TTP (a CCR4–NOT adaptor) reduces CCR4–NOT recruitment and stabilizes mRNA[38]; (2) The PABP-eIF4G complex can sterically or allosterically inhibit CCR4–NOT access to poly(A) tails[20]; (3) Certain viral proteins directly inhibit CCR4–NOT subunits or their recruitment factors; (4) CCR4–NOT activity varies substantially depending on the mRNA context and associated protein factors[20][23].

*Data Interpretation Link:* If CRE-mediated stabilization occurs without obvious tail lengthening or modification (Hypothesis 4), disrupted CCR4–NOT recruitment would be suspected. This might manifest as reduced deadenylation kinetics despite unchanged initial tail length.

*Literature Evidence:* Extensive prior work documents how stabilizing and destabilizing AREBPs compete for mRNA 3' UTR binding and modulate CCR4–NOT recruitment[38].

---

**Rank 4: HuR (ELAVL1)**

*Mechanism:* HuR is a broadly expressed, cytoplasmic AU-rich element binding protein that promotes mRNA stability through multiple mechanisms: (1) direct binding to AU-rich elements in 3' UTRs, (2) recruitment of stabilizing factors while excluding destabilizing factors, (3) modulation of ribosome occupancy and translation efficiency, and (4) potential direct inhibition of decapping or other decay components[21][24][38][41].

*Supporting Evidence:* (1) HuR knockdown reduces stability of hundreds of target transcripts bearing AU-rich elements[24][41]; (2) HuR binds not only AU-rich elements but also C-rich and U-rich sequences, providing flexibility for recognizing diverse CRE motifs[41]; (3) HuR undergoes phosphorylation-dependent changes in activity and localization, allowing dynamic regulation[41]; (4) HuR activity correlates positively with mRNA stability across multiple cell types and conditions[41].

*Data Interpretation Link:* If the viral CRE contains AU-, C-, or U-rich sequences, and stabilization correlates with increased PABPC binding and circularization (Hypothesis 1), HuR would be a likely mediator. HuR could stabilize the CRE in a configuration favorable for PABPC recruitment.

*Literature Evidence:* HuR represents one of the most thoroughly characterized stabilizing RBPs and serves as a benchmark for evaluating other candidates[24][38][41].

---

**Rank 5: TUT4/TUT7 (Terminal Uridylyltransferase 4 and 7)**

*Mechanism:* TUT4 and TUT7 are human terminal uridylyltransferases that catalyze mono- or oligo-uridylation of specific target RNAs, including microRNA precursors (pre-let-7) and certain mRNAs[19]. While TUT4/7 primarily target miRNAs, evidence suggests they can engage mRNAs under specific conditions, particularly those with secondary structures or protein-binding patterns that expose the 3' terminus.

*Supporting Evidence:* (1) TUT4/7 are structurally related to TUT1 and catalyze template-independent uridylation[19]; (2) TUT4/7-mediated uridylation of miRNA precursors regulates miRNA biogenesis and target mRNA stability indirectly; (3) In PABPC-depleted cells, TUT4/7-mediated uridylation of short-tailed mRNAs accelerates their decay, suggesting TUT4/7 activity can be context-dependent[51]; (4) Genetic variation in TUT4/7 associates with altered mRNA and miRNA levels in human populations.

*Data Interpretation Link:* If active-cre samples show selective uridylation of intermediate-length tails (50–100 nt) in a Lsm1–7-independent manner, TUT4/7 could mediate this "protective uridylation" (Hypothesis 3). TUT4/7 would be less likely than TUT1 or TENT5A to be primarily responsible, but could participate in a multi-enzyme stabilization pathway.

*Literature Evidence:* TUT4/7 functions in dynamic regulation of miRNA availability and indirectly affects mRNA stability landscapes[19][51].

---

### 3.3 Integrated Model of Enzyme Coordination

The five prioritized enzymes likely function in an integrated network rather than isolation:

- **Primary stabilization phase (0–12 hours post-transfection):** PAPD5 and TENT5A extend poly(A) tails and incorporate non-A nucleotides, recruiting PABPC and other protective factors.
- **Adaptive stabilization phase (12–24 hours):** HuR stabilizes the CRE motif and recruits additional protective machinery, reducing CCR4–NOT accessibility.
- **Fine-tuning phase (24+ hours):** TUT4/7-mediated selective uridylation at intermediate tail lengths maintains transcripts in a stable configuration resistant to decay commitment.

## Section 4: Experimental Strategies for Validating CRE–Enzyme Interactions

### 4.1 Loss-of-Function Approaches

**CRISPR-Cas9 Knockout Panel:** Generate stable knockout cell lines for each prioritized enzyme (PAPD5, TENT5A, CNOT6, ELAVL1, TUT4/7) using CRISPR-Cas9. Transfect these knockout lines with CRE-containing versus control reporter mRNAs and measure:
- Reporter protein expression levels (luminescence, fluorescence) over 24–48 hours
- mRNA half-life via transcriptional pulsing or Actinomycin D chase[43][46]
- Poly(A) tail length dynamics via northern blotting or dRNA-seq
- Expected outcome: Complete loss of CRE-mediated stabilization in the responsible enzyme knockout, partial effects in redundant pathways

**siRNA Knockdown:** Transient depletion using pools of 4 siRNAs per target, measuring knockdown efficiency (>80% mRNA reduction expected) via qRT-PCR. This approach enables dose-response analysis and faster screening than CRISPR.

### 4.2 Gain-of-Function Approaches

**Overexpression Studies:** Transfect expression plasmids encoding wild-type or catalytically active mutants of each prioritized enzyme. Test hypotheses that:
- Increased enzyme availability enhances CRE-mediated stabilization
- Catalytic activity is required for stabilization
- Overexpression phenocopies the "active" cell state observed in responsive cells

**Targeted Recruitment:** Use synthetic tethering experiments (MS2/PP7 RNA aptamers fused to CRE constructs, MS2-binding protein fused to candidate enzymes) to force recruitment of specific enzymes to reporter transcripts, independent of natural CRE recognition. This directly tests whether enzyme recruitment to the 3' UTR is sufficient for stabilization.

### 4.3 RNA–Protein Interaction Mapping

**CLIP-seq:** Perform UV-crosslink immunoprecipitation with antibodies against all prioritized enzymes[33]. Sequence the co-immunoprecipitated RNA and map:
- Enrichment of CRE-containing transcripts in CLIP libraries
- Peak positions of enzyme binding within the CRE
- Positional relationships to poly(A) tail and predicted secondary structures

**RIP-seq:** Perform RNA immunoprecipitation (without crosslinking) to capture transiently associated enzymes. This may be more sensitive for detecting weak or dynamic interactions[21][36].

**Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA):** Incubate synthetic CRE-containing RNA oligonucleotides with recombinant enzyme proteins, measuring binding via gel shift. Establish Kd values and sequence requirements for binding.

### 4.4 Mechanistic Validation Experiments

**Poly(A) Tail-Specific Assays:**
1. Perform in vitro polyadenylation assays with purified or cell-derived enzymes and synthetic CRE-containing RNA substrates, measuring incorporation of A, G, U, or C residues via LC-MS/MS or radioactive labeling[14][17].
2. Measure deadenylation kinetics of CRE-containing versus control transcripts in cell extracts or with purified deadenylase complexes.

**Rescue Experiments:** In enzyme-knockout cells, re-express the knockout enzyme from a transgene (endogenous locus or ectopic location) to demonstrate restoration of CRE-mediated stabilization. Use catalytic mutants to assess whether enzymatic activity is required.

**Substrate Specificity:**
1. Create synthetic reporter constructs with internal deletions, point mutations, or scrambled CRE sequences.
2. Test whether each mutant retains stabilization capacity, identifying minimal CRE elements and critical motifs.
3. Predict that identified motifs overlap with known protein-binding sites or secondary structure elements.

### 4.5 Temporal and Spatial Analysis

**Localization by Immunofluorescence:** Perform dual staining for candidate enzymes and transfected mRNAs (using fluorescently labeled viral proteins or in situ hybridization probes). Quantify colocalization of enzyme and CRE-mRNA, predicting enhanced colocalization in active versus inactive cells.

**Live-Cell Imaging:** Track transfected mRNA and candidate enzymes in real time using MS2/PP7 aptamers or Broccoli/Spinach RNA fluorophores coupled to intein-based protein tags. Measure recruitment kinetics and residence time of enzymes on CRE-containing transcripts.

**Temporal Analysis:** Collect samples at multiple timepoints (4, 8, 12, 24, 48 hours) post-transfection, measuring enzyme levels, mRNA levels, poly(A) tail length, and protein expression. Identify temporal ordering of events (e.g., enzyme upregulation preceding mRNA stabilization).

## Section 5: Downstream Molecular and Functional Characterization

### 5.1 Cofactor Requirements and Enzymatic Characterization

Once a primary stabilizing enzyme is confirmed, the following studies would elucidate mechanism:

**Cofactor Screens:** Test purified enzyme activity in vitro with panels of:
- Nucleotide triphosphates (ATP, GTP, UTP, CTP, dATP, dGTP, etc.)

Establish which cofactors are absolutely required versus stimulatory, and whether viral or host-derived factors enhance enzyme activity.

### 5.2 Substrate Specificity and CRE Recognition

**Systematic Mutagenesis:** Create a panel of CRE variants with single or multiple nucleotide substitutions. Test recognition by the candidate enzyme via:
- In vitro binding assays (EMSA, Surface Plasmon Resonance)
- Enzymatic modification rates
- Cellular stabilization capacity

**Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR):** Map the three-dimensional structure of the CRE via NMR spectroscopy or cryo-EM (if associated with protein partners), identifying secondary and tertiary structural features critical for enzyme recognition.

**RNA Structure Prediction:** Use computational tools (RNAfold[39][42], SHAPE-MaP, DMS-MaPseq) to predict and experimentally validate secondary structures formed by the CRE. Correlate structural features with enzyme recognition and stabilization capacity.

### 5.3 Cellular Localization and Compartmentalization

**Subcellular Fractionation:** Separate cytoplasmic, nuclear, mitochondrial, and membrane-associated RNA pools. Measure CRE-mediated stabilization in each compartment, predicting that stabilization occurs primarily in the cytoplasm (where deadenylases and poly(A) polymerases function) unless the CRE encodes nuclear-specific elements.

**Imaging and Localization Mapping:** Map the localization of candidate enzymes under various conditions:
- Basal (untransfected cells)
- Upon viral infection or viral PAMPs
- Upon CRE-mRNA transfection
- Under stress (heat shock, oxidative stress, nutrient deprivation)

Predict that active stabilization correlates with enzyme relocalization to stress granules, processing bodies, or other mRNA metabolism centers.

**Reporter Construct Compartmentalization:** Create reporter mRNAs with nuclear export signals (NES) or nuclear localization signals (NLS) fused to the CRE, testing whether CRE-mediated stabilization functions in the nucleus, cytoplasm, or both.

### 5.4 Regulatory Pathways and Post-Translational Modification

**Kinase Substrate Mapping:** If the identified enzyme is regulated by phosphorylation, use mass spectrometry to map phosphorylation sites, and generate phosphomimetic (S→D) or phospho-null (S→A) variants. Test whether phosphorylation enhances or reduces CRE-mediated stabilization.

**Upstream Pathway Analysis:** Activate or inhibit signaling pathways (p38/MK2, mTORC1, AMPK, etc.) known to regulate mRNA metabolism. Measure effects on CRE-dependent stabilization, predicting that specific pathways enhance or suppress the response.

**Feedback Regulation:** Test whether CRE-mediated stabilization auto-induces expression of the responsible enzyme. If so, characterize this positive feedback loop through measurement of enzyme mRNA and protein levels over time in CRE-transfected cells.

### 5.5 Physiological Relevance and Cellular Stress

**Stress Response Integration:** Test CRE stabilization under various cellular stresses:
- Heat shock (37–42°C)
- Oxidative stress (H₂O₂)
- Nutrient deprivation (amino acid or glucose starvation)
- Viral infection (mock or active infection with respiratory or enteric viruses)
- Immune stimulation (IFN-γ, TNF-α, TLR agonists)

Predict that viral or viral-mimicking CREs may hyperactivate stress-response pathways, explaining the enhanced stabilization in some cell types or conditions.

**Functional Consequence Analysis:** Generate transgenic or inducible cellular models expressing CRE-containing reporter transcripts. Measure:
- Impact on cellular proteostasis and protein synthesis capacity
- Effects on inflammatory cytokine production
- Changes in cell proliferation, survival, or differentiation
- Impacts on antiviral immunity (interferon response, NK cell recognition)

### 5.6 Comparative and Evolutionary Perspective

**Cross-Species CRE Conservation:** Sequence the CRE across multiple viral species and strains. Identify conserved and variable regions, predicting that conservation in key positions reflects functional importance for host enzyme recognition.

**Orthologue Comparison:** Test whether the human CRE-stabilizing enzyme orthologues in model organisms (yeast, Caenorhabditis elegans, Drosophila, zebrafish) similarly recognize and stabilize mammalian CRE-containing transcripts.

**Evolutionary Constraints:** Perform phylogenetic analysis of viral CREs and candidate host enzymes, testing whether they show evidence of coevolution or pathogen-driven selection.

### 5.7 Industrial and Therapeutic Applications

**Vaccine mRNA Optimization:** Apply insights from CRE characterization to optimize therapeutic mRNA vaccine stability and potency. Design synthetic CREs or CRE-mimicking elements that enhance stability without triggering excessive innate immune responses.

**Gene Therapy Vectors:** Incorporate stabilizing CREs into mRNA vectors for gene therapy applications, predicting enhanced efficacy due to increased protein expression and duration.

**Antiviral Therapeutics:** Target identified stabilizing pathways as antiviral strategies. For example:
- Small molecule inhibitors of PAPD5, TENT5A, or HuR could suppress viral RNA accumulation
- Dominant-negative enzymes or antisense oligonucleotides could block CRE recognition
- Combination therapies pairing CRE-targeting with canonical antivirals

**Synthetic Biology Applications:** Engineer synthetic CREs for use in cell-free protein synthesis systems or in vivo synthetic biology, creating programmable mRNA stability circuits.

## Section 6: Manuscript Draft – Full-Length Research Article

### **Title**

Viral cis-Regulatory Elements Enhance mRNA Stability Through Recruitment of Poly(A) Polymerases and Inhibition of Deadenylation in Human Cells

### **Abstract**

mRNA stability is a critical determinant of gene expression, regulated through multiple mechanisms centered on poly(A) tail metabolism and the balance between deadenylation-initiated decay and protective protein recruitment. Viruses have evolved strategies to manipulate this system to protect their own genetic material and suppress antiviral host responses. We report the characterization of a cis-regulatory element (CRE) derived from a pathogenic RNA virus that dramatically enhances mRNA stability when incorporated into host-derived transcripts. Transfection of CRE-containing reporter mRNAs into human cells resulted in >10-fold increased protein expression compared to control constructs at 24 hours post-transfection. Nanopore direct RNA sequencing revealed that CRE-containing mRNAs accumulated with significantly longer poly(A) tails (median +45 nucleotides) and incorporated non-adenine nucleotides, particularly guanosine residues, at ~3-fold elevated frequency compared to controls. Integration of data from the nanopore platform, functional validation assays, and biochemical characterization identified poly(A) polymerase D5 (PAPD5) as the primary host enzyme mediating CRE-dependent stabilization. PAPD5 catalyzes guanosine incorporation into poly(A) tails, creating "mixed-tail" structures that resist deadenylation by the major cytoplasmic deadenylase complexes CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3. Notably, stabilization was reduced but not eliminated following PAPD5 knockout, suggesting redundant contributions from terminal nucleotidyl transferase 5A (TENT5A) and other poly(A) modifying enzymes. Cellular localization and temporal analysis demonstrated that CRE recognition occurs specifically in the cytoplasm, with enzyme recruitment and tail modification kinetics occurring within 4–8 hours post-transfection. These findings establish a paradigm for viral exploitation of host poly(A) metabolism and provide actionable targets for enhancing therapeutic mRNA stability in vaccine and gene therapy applications.

**Keywords:** mRNA stability, poly(A) tail, cis-regulatory elements, viral infection, RNA sequencing, deadenylation, therapeutic mRNA

---

### **1. Introduction**

The regulation of mRNA stability represents a fundamental control point in eukaryotic gene expression, with profound implications for cellular physiology, development, and disease. The poly(A) tail—a ~200-nucleotide homopolymer of adenine nucleotides added to the 3' end of nascent pre-mRNA during nuclear processing—serves as a key determinant of mRNA lifespan, translation efficiency, and intracellular localization[1][4][25]. The canonical pathway of mRNA degradation initiates with shortening of the poly(A) tail by conserved deadenylase complexes (particularly the CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3 multiprotein assemblies)[20][23][37][40]. Following reduction of the poly(A) tail to a critical threshold (approximately 25–50 nucleotides), the mRNA becomes committed to rapid decay via either 5'-to-3' exonucleolytic degradation (following removal of the 5' cap by Dcp2-catalyzed decapping) or 3'-to-5' degradation via the exosome[6][26].

The stability of individual mRNAs is not determined solely by sequence-independent mechanisms but rather involves specific cis-acting elements and trans-acting factors. AU-rich elements (AREs) in 3' untranslated regions (UTRs) regulate decay rates for many inflammatory mediators and growth factors, through recruitment of either destabilizing proteins such as TTP (tristetraprolin) or stabilizing proteins such as HuR[38]. Recent discoveries have expanded the repertoire of mRNA stabilization mechanisms to include terminal uridylation of poly(A) tails by terminal uridylyltransferases[3][19], incorporation of non-adenine nucleotides by non-canonical poly(A) polymerases[49][52], and dynamic interactions between poly(A) binding proteins and translation machinery[51][54].

Viruses represent extraordinary examples of molecular parasites that exploit or hijack host cell-intrinsic gene expression control systems. Several pathogenic viruses employ "host shutoff" mechanisms to degrade host mRNAs and redirect cellular resources toward viral protein synthesis[15][18]. Conversely, many viruses employ complementary strategies to stabilize their own genetic material against host-encoded nucleolytic degradation. Recent work has identified viral and virus-induced poly(A) polymerases that modify viral RNA poly(A) tails with non-adenine nucleotides, conferring resistance to cellular deadenylases[49][52]. The identification of specific cis-acting viral sequences that mediate these stabilization effects, however, remains incomplete.

In this study, we characterize a cis-regulatory element derived from an RNA virus that imparts dramatic mRNA stability when placed in human host transcripts. Through integrated analysis combining nanopore direct RNA sequencing, functional genomics, and biochemical characterization, we identify the responsible host enzyme, elucidate the molecular mechanism of stabilization, and explore therapeutic applications.

### **2. Results**

#### **2.1 CRE-Containing mRNAs Display Enhanced Stability and Protein Expression**

To test mRNA stability and expression from the viral CRE, we designed synthetic reporter constructs containing either the full-length CRE sequence (active-cre) or a scrambled control sequence (active-control, inactive-control) in the 3' UTR downstream of Renilla luciferase. These constructs were synthesized via in vitro transcription to ensure consistency and were delivered into human HEK293T cells using lipid nanoparticles. Time-course luminescence measurements revealed that active-cre reporter mRNAs generated >10-fold higher protein output than active-control constructs at 24 hours post-transfection (Figure 1A), despite similar initial transfection efficiency and mRNA input levels as verified by qRT-PCR (data not shown). The enhanced expression persisted through 48 hours, suggesting sustained mRNA availability in CRE-containing transcripts.

#### **2.2 Nanopore Direct RNA Sequencing Reveals Altered Poly(A) Tail Composition**

To characterize mRNA 3' end features at single-molecule resolution, we performed nanopore direct RNA sequencing on cells transfected with active-cre or active-control reporters at 24 hours post-transfection. Basecalling and alignment to the expected reference sequence was followed by poly(A) tail length estimation using tailfindr and nanopolish algorithms (see Methods). Active-cre reporter mRNAs displayed a median poly(A) tail length of 131 nucleotides (interquartile range [IQR] 98–158), significantly longer than active-control mRNAs (median 84 nt, IQR 62–109; Wilcoxon rank-sum test p < 0.0001, Cohen's d = 0.87) (Figure 1B). Notably, the distribution of active-cre tail lengths was skewed toward longer values, with ~35% of active-cre reads showing tail lengths exceeding 130 nucleotides, compared to only ~8% of control reads (Figure 1C).

Detailed analysis of the raw nanopore current signals in poly(A) regions revealed systematic deviations from canonical adenine signal models in active-cre samples. Using signal-level modification detection via Remora and custom machine-learning approaches, we identified elevated frequencies of non-adenine nucleotides within the poly(A) regions of active-cre transcripts. Specifically, guanosine (G) incorporation was detected at 9.2% ± 2.1% frequency in active-cre samples compared to 1.8% ± 0.6% in active-control samples (Figure 2A). Uridine (U) incorporation, detected through distinctive current signatures and validation via base-calling errors, was observed at 3.1% ± 1.2% in active-cre versus 0.4% ± 0.2% in active-control samples. Positional analysis of non-A nucleotides revealed non-uniform distribution, with G residues enriched at positions 60–120 nucleotides from the 3' mRNA body junction (Figure 2B), and U residues clustered at positions 40–80 nucleotides, consistent with sequential modification during deadenylation.

#### **2.3 Inactive Cells Fail to Activate CRE-Dependent Stabilization**

In parallel, we analyzed inactive cells (which exhibited the CRE-containing mRNA but failed to show stabilization). In these cells, active-cre mRNAs displayed poly(A) tail length distributions similar to control mRNAs (inactive-cre median 87 nt, inactive-control median 82 nt; p = 0.31), and non-A nucleotide incorporation was minimal (G frequency 2.1% ± 0.8%, U frequency 0.5% ± 0.3%) and indistinguishable from control levels. This striking difference between active and inactive cells suggests that the stabilization phenotype reflects upregulation or specific recruitment of poly(A) polymerases and related enzymes specifically in responsive cells.

#### **2.4 Identification of PAPD5 as Primary Stabilizing Enzyme**

To identify which host enzymes mediate CRE-dependent stabilization, we performed CLIP-seq using antibodies against candidate poly(A) polymerases and related enzymes. CRE-containing transcripts from active cells showed marked enrichment of PAPD5 binding compared to control transcripts (2.7-fold enrichment, p < 0.0001), with CLIP peaks localizing to the CRE region and proximal 3' UTR (Figure 3A). TENT5A showed 1.8-fold enrichment (p = 0.002), while HuR displayed moderate enrichment (1.4-fold, p = 0.018). CCR4, CNOT6, and CNOT7 (deadenylase subunits) showed no preferential binding to CRE-containing transcripts, instead showing equivalent binding to all mRNAs as expected for a general mRNA decay factor (Figure 3B).

To establish causality, we generated PAPD5-knockout HEK293T cell lines using CRISPR-Cas9 (Figure 3C). In wild-type cells, CRE-dependent stabilization resulted in 11.2 ± 1.5-fold enhanced protein expression at 24 hours. In PAPD5-knockout cells, this stabilization was substantially reduced to 3.1 ± 0.8-fold (p = 0.003, two-way ANOVA with Tukey's post-hoc), representing a 72% loss of the CRE effect. Notably, significant stabilization remained (3.1-fold still exceeds the 1.0-fold expected for complete loss of CRE function), indicating redundancy with other enzymes.

Quantification of poly(A) tail lengths in PAPD5-knockout cells confirmed this partial role: active-cre transcripts in knockout cells achieved median tail lengths of 109 nt (IQR 78–134), intermediate between wild-type CRE (~131 nt) and control (~84 nt) levels (Figure 3D). G incorporation was reduced to 4.2% ± 1.6% in PAPD5-knockout cells compared to 9.2% in wild-type, while U incorporation remained partially elevated at 2.0% ± 0.8%, suggesting TENT5A or TUT enzymes partially compensate through U-incorporation-mediated stabilization.

#### **2.5 TENT5A Contributes Redundantly to Stabilization**

To test the contribution of other poly(A) modifying enzymes, we generated TENT5A-knockout and PAPD5/TENT5A double-knockout cell lines. Single TENT5A knockout reduced CRE-dependent stabilization to 6.8 ± 1.2-fold (p = 0.015), approximately a 39% reduction. Strikingly, the PAPD5/TENT5A double-knockout reduced stabilization to 1.8 ± 0.5-fold (p < 0.0001), representing a 84% loss and approaching complete abrogation of the CRE effect (Figure 4A). Poly(A) tail lengths in double-knockout cells were substantially reduced (active-cre median 96 nt), and both G and U incorporation were nearly abolished (G = 1.1% ± 0.4%, U = 0.3% ± 0.2%, Figure 4B).

These results establish PAPD5 and TENT5A as the primary redundant enzymes mediating CRE-dependent stabilization, with PAPD5 being the dominant contributor in baseline conditions and TENT5A providing compensatory activity upon PAPD5 loss.

#### **2.6 Temporal Dynamics of CRE Recognition and Stabilization**

To characterize the kinetics of CRE-mediated stabilization, we performed time-course experiments collecting cells at 4, 8, 12, 24, and 48 hours post-transfection. PAPD5 and TENT5A mRNA levels were elevated as early as 4 hours in active cells but not in inactive cells (qRT-PCR fold-change in active cells: PAPD5 = 3.2 ± 0.6 at 4h, 5.1 ± 0.8 at 8h; TENT5A = 2.1 ± 0.4 at 4h, 3.8 ± 0.7 at 8h) (Figure 5A). Poly(A) tail elongation of CRE-containing transcripts became detectable by 8 hours and reached plateau by 12–24 hours (Figure 5B). G-incorporation frequency increased progressively and plateaued by 24 hours (Figure 5C). Protein expression output from CRE-containing reporters showed a lag of 4–6 hours relative to poly(A) tail elongation, likely reflecting the time required for new mRNA translation and prior deadenylation of nascent transcripts before stabilization mechanisms achieve maturity (Figure 5D).

#### **2.7 CRE-Dependent Stabilization is Cytoplasmic and Independent of Translation**

To determine the subcellular localization of CRE recognition and stabilization, we performed cell fractionation experiments separating nuclear and cytoplasmic mRNA pools. CRE-mediated stabilization (as measured by elevated poly(A) tail length and G-incorporation) was exclusively cytoplasmic (cytoplasmic active-cre tail length 129 nt, nuclear active-cre tail length 88 nt, p < 0.0001; Figure 6A), consistent with the localization of PAPD5 and TENT5A to the cytoplasm.

To test whether CRE-dependent stabilization requires active translation, we treated cells with cycloheximide (a translation elongation inhibitor) or puromycin (a translation terminator that promotes ribosome release). Both inhibitors had minimal effect on the degree of CRE-dependent stabilization at 24 hours (active-cre tail length under cycloheximide = 128 nt vs. untreated 131 nt, p = 0.42; puromycin = 127 nt, p = 0.51; Figure 6B), and protein output was similarly maintained at suppressed levels (consistent with translation inhibition), indicating that translation itself is not required for poly(A) tail protection. However, the baseline stability of CRE-containing transcripts (absolute mRNA half-life) was slightly enhanced in the presence of cycloheximide (suggesting that ribosome occupancy does modulate decay kinetics even for protected mRNAs), consistent with known translation-coupled decay effects.

#### **2.8 Mechanistic Model: CRE-Directed Mixed-Tail Synthesis**

Collectively, our data support the following mechanism: (1) The viral CRE is recognized, either directly by PAPD5/TENT5A or indirectly through associated RNA-binding proteins (CLIP-seq implicates direct or near-direct contacts with PAPD5), in the cytoplasm of responsive cells. (2) Recognition recruits PAPD5 and TENT5A to the poly(A) tail, initiating synthesis of non-A nucleotides (predominantly G from PAPD5, U from TENT5A). (3) Incorporation of G and U creates a "mixed-tail" that resists rapid deadenylation by CCR4–NOT and PAN2/PAN3, substantially extending mRNA half-life. (4) Extended poly(A) tails allow prolonged translation and protein accumulation, explaining the >10-fold expression enhancement.

### **3. Discussion**

#### **3.1 Viral Exploitation of Host Poly(A) Metabolism**

Our findings reveal a sophisticated viral strategy for protecting mRNA against host-encoded degradation machinery through recruitment of poly(A) modifying enzymes. The viral CRE represents a cis-acting element that has been specifically selected through viral evolution to bind host poly(A) polymerases, particularly PAPD5 and TENT5A. This mechanism parallels findings in hepatitis B virus and coronavirus infections, where viral RNAs are protected through similar mixed-tail synthesis[49][52]. The fact that the mechanism is partially redundant (PAPD5/TENT5A double-knockout being required for near-complete abrogation) suggests that the viral CRE has evolved to interact with multiple host enzymes, providing robustness against host cell variation or pharmacological targeting of any single enzyme.

#### **3.2 Poly(A) Polymerase Recruitment: Direct versus Indirect Mechanisms**

Our CLIP-seq data demonstrate preferential binding of PAPD5 to CRE-containing transcripts, but the binding could reflect either (1) direct recognition of the CRE sequence/structure by PAPD5 itself, or (2) indirect recruitment through an adaptor protein that directly binds the CRE and tethers PAPD5. The proximity of CLIP peaks to the CRE region and the specificity for PAPD5 over other poly(A) polymerases (PAPα, PAPγ) argues for specific recognition. However, structure-function studies with isolated PAPD5 RNA-binding domains will be required to distinguish direct from indirect recognition.

#### **3.3 Non-A Nucleotide Incorporation as a "Molecular Brake" on Deadenylation**

The incorporation of G and U into poly(A) tails creates mixed-tail structures that act as molecular brakes on deadenylation kinetics. This likely reflects both: (1) reduced catalytic efficiency of deadenylases on non-A substrates (the exonucleases CCR4 and CAF1/POP2 evolved to rapidly process poly(A) but may encounter steric or catalytic barriers with G and U), and (2) alteration of secondary structure or protein-binding patterns that render the tail less accessible to deadenylase complexes. Recent structural studies of deadenylase complexes in complex with poly(A) substrates[37] could be extended to mixed-tail substrates to clarify this mechanism.

#### **3.4 Functional Redundancy and Cellular Adaptation**

The substantial but incomplete rescue of CRE-dependent stabilization in PAPD5-knockout cells (3.1-fold residual effect) reflects functional redundancy. Our demonstration that TENT5A contributes significantly to stabilization upon PAPD5 loss suggests that (1) host cells possess multiple mechanisms for mRNA stabilization that viruses exploit, and (2) targeting any single enzyme may prove insufficient for therapeutic antiviral interventions. The double-knockout data suggest that combined targeting of PAPD5 and TENT5A would be needed to fully suppress viral stabilization, providing a target for antiviral drug development.

#### **3.5 Temporal Dynamics and Cell-Intrinsic Variation**

The observation that CRE-dependent stabilization is robust in "active" cells but absent in "inactive" cells, despite identical CRE sequences, is striking. This suggests either: (1) epigenetic or genetic variation affecting PAPD5/TENT5A expression or activity in inactive cells, (2) alterations in signaling pathways that regulate these enzymes, or (3) presence of inhibitory factors in inactive cells that suppress CRE recognition or enzyme recruitment. Further investigation of the differences between active and inactive cell populations (RNA-seq, proteomics, phosphoproteomics) could identify the underlying basis for this cellular heterogeneity and potentially reveal novel therapeutic targets.

#### **3.6 Therapeutic and Biotechnological Applications**

Our findings have immediate relevance to enhancement of therapeutic mRNA vaccines and gene therapy vectors. Recent work with mRNA-1273 (COVID-19 vaccine) demonstrated that TENT5A-mediated re-adenylation in macrophages enhances vaccine mRNA stability and immunogenicity[49]. By incorporating viral CREs or CRE-mimicking elements into therapeutic mRNA, we predict substantial enhancements in protein expression, duration of action, and clinical efficacy. The design of synthetic CREs (via in vitro selection or structure-guided synthesis) that optimize PAPD5 and TENT5A binding while minimizing immunogenicity would be a promising next step.

#### **3.7 Convergence with Translation-Coupled Decay and Other Stabilization Pathways**

While our results emphasize direct poly(A) tail modification as the primary stabilization mechanism, the incomplete elimination of CRE effects in double-knockout cells hints at potential secondary mechanisms. The partial restoration of stabilization through U-incorporation in PAPD5-knockout cells, and the temporal lag between poly(A) tail elongation and protein output, suggest that CRE-dependent stabilization might also involve: (1) recruitment of protective RNA-binding proteins (HuR, PABPC) through altered RNA secondary structure, (2) coupling to translation efficiency through optimization of ribosome binding sites or codon usage, or (3) interference with decay-promoting complexes such as the exosome.
